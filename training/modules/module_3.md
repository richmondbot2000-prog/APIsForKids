# Module 3 — Bystander Action: The 5Ds

**Duration:** ~10 minutes reading + 8-question test
**Pass mark:** 80% (7 of 8). Two retakes permitted before HR escalation.

---

## Section 1: Why this module exists

Roughly 95% of the people taking this training will never harass anyone. The previous two modules are mostly about the other 5%, and they had to be. This one is about you.

Almost everyone, at some point in a finance career, will witness or hear about something that sat oddly with them. A comment on a desk. A hand on a back at the bar. A WhatsApp image in a chat that should never have been sent. A pattern of someone being talked over in every meeting. The legal and regulatory framework in Module 1 only works if those moments produce a response from the people who saw them. The evidence is clear that they often don't — not because witnesses are indifferent, but because nobody equipped them.

This module is the equipment. It is **not** another reminder not to harass. It is a small, repeatable set of options for the situation you didn't expect to be in.

The framework is the **5Ds**: Direct, Distract, Delegate, Delay, Document. It originated with Green Dot at the University of Kentucky and was extended into its current form by Hollaback! / Right To Be. It is now the de facto bystander framework across UK universities and post–Worker Protection Act providers. We use it because the evidence behind it is the strongest in the field — multi-site randomised trials have shown 17–21% reductions in interpersonal-violence victimisation in populations that received it. No other intervention in the harassment-prevention literature shows that range of effect.

## Section 2: Direct

Saying something, in the moment, to the person doing the thing.

Direct intervention is the option most people imagine when they hear "bystander training," and the one most people overestimate the difficulty of. It does not require a confrontation. The version that works in a UK finance setting is short, factual, and unflustered:

- **Name the behaviour, not the person.** "That comment isn't OK" lands. "You're a sexist" doesn't.
- **Keep it brief.** One sentence, normal tone, no speech. Long interventions invite arguments; short ones close the moment.
- **Don't speak for the affected colleague.** Ask, afterwards, what they want — they may want the matter dropped, escalated, or simply acknowledged. The bystander's job is to interrupt, not to take over.

Direct works best when the power asymmetry between you and the person doing the thing is small or in your favour, when you are confident you have read the situation correctly, and when the moment is still live. It works less well — and can backfire — across a steep hierarchy, in front of a client, or when alcohol has changed the room.

**[scenario]** On a Wednesday-morning team call, a senior trader makes a passing crack about a junior analyst "being too young to remember the last crisis, but cute trying." Direct, from a peer on the call, sounds like: "Let's keep it on the trade." That is enough. It names the behaviour by redirecting it, leaves the junior analyst's dignity intact without speaking for them, and takes about three seconds.

## Section 3: Distract

Changing the subject. Derailing the moment without confronting it.

Distract is the option for situations where Direct is too risky, too slow, or too likely to escalate. The mechanism is simple: you remove the conditions that allow the conduct to continue, by interrupting the flow of the moment with something innocuous.

- "Could you give me a hand with something for a sec?"
- "Sorry to interrupt — has anyone seen [X]?"
- "Let's grab a coffee, I need your read on something."

The point is not to be subtle. The point is to give the affected colleague an exit, or to give the person doing the thing a reason to stop without anyone losing face. Distract is particularly useful in informal settings — drinks, lunches, the kitchen, the bar after an off-site — where Direct intervention would be conspicuous and Delegate would be slow.

**[scenario]** At a client lunch, a contractor at the table makes a racist comment about a competitor's CEO. Direct intervention from a junior member of the team risks the client relationship; saying nothing risks the colleagues at the table. Distract is "I'm going to grab some water — back in a moment, can I get you anything?" addressed to the affected colleague, which both removes them from the table briefly and signals that you noticed.

## Section 4: Delegate

Finding someone better positioned to act.

Delegate is not a way of passing the problem along. It is recognising that the right intervention sometimes requires a particular relationship, a particular seniority, or a particular role — and that you may not be it. Delegate options include:

- The line manager of the person doing the thing.
- A senior in the room (a partner, a head of desk, an MD).
- HR, by phone the same day, not by email next week.
- Security, where physical safety is in play.
- The Speak Up route or the firm's whistleblowing channel, where the chain above is part of the problem.

A specific case of delegation that the literature underweights: **delegate to the affected colleague.** Sometimes the right thing is to step back, check in privately, and let them decide what they want done. Not every situation requires the bystander to act; some require the bystander to ask.

The instinct to "not make a fuss" by going to a manager is the instinct the firm needs you to override. Delegation is escalation done correctly — early, calm, and with names and times attached.

**[scenario]** At an off-site dinner, a member of senior leadership repeatedly puts a hand on a junior colleague's lower back at the bar. Direct from a peer is plausible but high-risk given the seniority involved. The cleaner intervention is Delegate: a quiet word to the most senior person in the room not in that exchange — the COO, the General Counsel, whoever it is — with one sentence ("I think you should know what's happening at the bar") and a name. They have the seniority to act; you have done the part that needed doing.

## Section 5: Delay

Circling back later.

Most workplace situations move fast. You will sometimes see something and only afterwards know what it was. You will sometimes freeze. You will sometimes be in a meeting that ended before you found the words. Delay is the recognition that **the moment passing does not end the obligation** — and, importantly, that delayed support is not lesser support.

The research is unambiguous on this: the single most valuable thing a bystander can do for an affected colleague is, often hours or days later, to say *I saw what happened. Are you OK?* That sentence does three things at once: it confirms the colleague isn't imagining it, it removes the isolation that makes harassment corrosive, and it creates the conditions in which the colleague can decide what they want to do next.

Concrete versions:

- "I noticed what happened in the meeting earlier. I didn't say anything at the time and I should have. Are you OK?"
- "That comment at lunch wasn't OK. I wanted to say so. Do you want to talk about it?"
- "I've been thinking about Tuesday's call. I'm sorry I didn't push back. What would be useful?"

Delay also includes following up *with yourself* — writing the moment down while it is fresh, deciding whether to raise it tomorrow, and not letting the discomfort of the moment metabolise into a reason to do nothing.

**[scenario]** A colleague returning from maternity leave is being talked over in every team meeting. No single instance crosses a clear line; the pattern is the issue. Direct and Distract are awkward in the meeting itself. Delay is right: a private message that evening — "I've noticed you're not getting much air time in the team meetings. Is that something you want me to push back on, or would you rather handle it yourself?" — gives the colleague the choice and signals that the pattern has been seen.

## Section 6: Document

Writing it down. Not later, contemporaneously.

The thing that separates "I think this happened" from "here is what happened" is a contemporaneous note. The legal system, the regulator, the firm's own investigation process, and ultimately the affected colleague all depend on documentation. Witnesses whose recollection is clear, dated, and consistent are the single most valuable input to a harassment investigation. Witnesses whose recollection is "a few weeks ago, I think, something around that time" are not useless, but they are dramatically weaker.

What to capture:

- **Date and time** — to the hour, ideally.
- **Location** — meeting, channel, venue, room.
- **Who was present** — by name where you can.
- **What was said or done** — direct quotes in inverted commas where possible, paraphrase elsewhere; mark clearly which is which.
- **What you did** — including "nothing in the moment" if that is the truth. Honest notes are stronger than flattering ones.

How to capture it:

- A **timestamped note in your phone's notes app, or an email to yourself**, is the cleanest form. Both produce a verifiable creation date.
- A handwritten note in a dated notebook is acceptable.
- **Screenshots are evidence of a kind, but chain-of-custody matters.** A WhatsApp screenshot can be edited without any audit trail; the original chat history on the original device carries WhatsApp's own integrity markers, timestamps, and (since 2023) an "edited" tag on any message changed within the 15-minute edit window — context a screenshot strips out. Where the conduct is in a digital channel, preserve the message itself in place — don't delete the chat, don't leave the group. The screenshot is your aide-memoire; the message is the evidence.
- Do **not** circulate documentation. The note is for you, for HR, for the affected colleague if they ask, and ultimately for an investigator. It is not for your team WhatsApp.

Documentation is the D that requires the least courage and produces the most leverage. If you do nothing else from this module, do this one.

**[scenario]** A WhatsApp group chat with photos from an off-site contains an image that is plainly leering — a long-lens shot of a colleague at the pool, taken without their knowledge. The right Document response is a screenshot of the image and the surrounding messages, with date and time visible, saved to a private location. The chat itself should not be deleted by anyone. The next D — Delegate, to HR — follows the same day.

## Section 7: The meta-fear — "Will this hurt my career?"

The honest answer is that the firm cannot promise no consequence, because the firm does not control every reaction of every individual. What the firm can do — and what the law has now made unambiguous — is that any consequence directed at you because you raised, supported, or witnessed a concern is itself a separate unlawful act, with its own remedies.

Three pieces of law on this, all live as of today:

- **Section 27 of the Equality Act 2010 — victimisation.** Subjecting someone to a detriment because they have done a "protected act" — making a complaint, supporting a complaint, giving evidence, or even alleging that the Act has been contravened — is itself unlawful. The original complaint does not have to be upheld. The retaliation is actionable in its own right.
- **April 2026 — protected disclosure status for sexual-harassment whistleblowing.** Under section 23 of the Employment Rights Act 2025, a qualifying disclosure relating to sexual harassment is now a protected disclosure under the whistleblowing regime. Detriment or dismissal in response carries **uncapped compensation** in the employment tribunal — on top of any Equality Act claim. The public-interest test still applies, so an individual personal grievance may not always qualify, but anything touching a pattern, a repeat offender, or a systemic failure will.
- **FCA SMCR overlay.** From September 2026, victimising or retaliating against a colleague for raising a non-financial misconduct concern is itself capable of being a Conduct Rule 1 (integrity) breach. The person doing the retaliating, if they are an SMF or certified, puts their own fitness and propriety in scope.

The practical version, for the bystander deciding whether to act:

- Document what you saw and what you did. Documentation is itself a defence against later "you imagined it" framing.
- Where you raise something, raise it in writing where possible, even if the substantive conversation is in person. A short email saying "as discussed today" creates the timestamp.
- If you experience any negative consequence — a project withdrawn, a sudden performance concern that wasn't there last week, a pattern of being left off invites — that is the moment to escalate again, not to retreat. The retaliation pattern is well-documented and the law treats it as a separate wrong.

Career risk for bystanders who act in good faith is real but small, and is bounded by law that the regulator and the EHRC are willing to enforce. The risk for bystanders who do nothing is, increasingly, also real — to the affected colleague, to the firm, and to the witness's own ability to look at themselves in the mirror six months later.

## Section 8: Choosing which D

There is no flowchart. There are three questions:

1. **How safe is intervention?** Physical, professional, relational. If safe → Direct is on the table. If not → Distract or Delegate.
2. **How asymmetrical is the power?** If you are a peer of the person doing the thing → Direct is plausible. If they are several levels above you → Delegate.
3. **How live is the moment?** If it is unfolding now → Direct, Distract, or Delegate. If the moment has passed → Delay. **In every case → Document.**

A working heuristic: **Direct if you can, Distract if you can't, Delegate if it's bigger than you, Delay if you missed it, Document always.**

The five are not mutually exclusive. The senior-leadership-at-the-bar scenario above involves Delegate in the moment, Delay the following morning (checking on the affected colleague), and Document throughout. Most real situations require two or three of the Ds in sequence, not one in isolation.

## Section 9: After the moment

Two audiences, both important.

**For the affected colleague.** The bystander research is consistent on one thing: *acknowledgement* is among the most valuable single things a witness can offer — confirming that the colleague isn't imagining it, that the moment was as off as they felt it was, that they are not alone with the memory. This is not a small thing. The most-cited reason victims of workplace harassment give for not reporting is the fear that no one will believe them or that they will be judged for raising it. A bystander saying *I saw it, I noticed, are you OK?* removes both of those barriers at once.

Beyond acknowledgement: ask what they want. Not what you think they should want. Some people want the matter escalated immediately; some want it documented and held; some want nothing done at all and just want the conversation. Their preference governs, with one exception — where the conduct is sufficiently serious that the firm has its own duty to act, the bystander cannot guarantee silence. Be honest about that boundary up front.

**For yourself.** Witnessing harassment is not free. People who saw something and acted, and people who saw something and didn't, both report measurable distress in the days afterwards. Talk to someone you trust. The Employee Assistance Programme is confidential and available. If you are an SMCR-certified individual and the incident touches your part of the business, write your own contemporaneous note — your future self may need it.

## Section 10: What to take from this module

- 95% of the audience for harassment training will never harass anyone. This module is for the witnesses, not the perpetrators.
- The 5Ds — **Direct, Distract, Delegate, Delay, Document** — are the de facto framework. Use the established names.
- **Direct** is briefer and less dramatic than people expect. Name the behaviour, not the person.
- **Distract** is for moments where Direct is too risky. It works in informal settings where Delegate would be too slow.
- **Delegate** is escalation done early and with names attached. Sometimes you delegate to the affected colleague themselves.
- **Delay** is the right move when the moment has passed. Delayed support is still support — often the most valuable kind.
- **Document** is the lowest-cost, highest-leverage D. Date, time, location, who was present, what was said, what you did. Notes app or email to self; preserve digital evidence in place; don't circulate.
- Victimisation for raising or supporting a concern is unlawful under s.27 Equality Act. Detriment for a sexual-harassment whistleblowing disclosure carries uncapped damages under the April 2026 reforms.
- After the moment, the single most valuable thing you can do for the affected colleague is acknowledge what you saw and ask what they want.

---

> **Plain-English legal box.** The 5Ds are a behavioural framework, not a legal duty — you have no personal legal obligation as a bystander to intervene. The firm has the duty (sections 40 and 40A of the Equality Act 2010, rising to "all reasonable steps" in October 2026), and the firm meets that duty in part by equipping you with options. Acting in good faith on the framework above will never be held against you. Retaliation against you for doing so is itself unlawful under section 27 of the Equality Act 2010 and, where the disclosure qualifies, under the whistleblowing regime as amended by section 23 of the Employment Rights Act 2025 (in force since 6 April 2026).
