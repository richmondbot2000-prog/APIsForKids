# Module 2 — Recognising harassment in an office setting

**Duration:** ~12 minutes reading + 8-question test

---

## Section 1: What this module is for

Module 1 taught the legal definition. This module makes it concrete.

The Equality Act 2010 protects nine characteristics, but only seven of them trigger the s.26 harassment provisions: **age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation**. The other two — **marriage and civil partnership** and **pregnancy and maternity** — sit outside s.26 but remain protected against direct discrimination. We come back to that at the end of the module.

What follows is one section per harassment-relevant characteristic. Each has a short definition, a plausible scenario from somewhere in the firm, and a grey area where reasonable people might genuinely disagree. The order is deliberate: we lead with the characteristics this audience is statistically most likely to wave away, and end with the ones the audience is most likely to take seriously on first reading.

One thing to absorb before we start. **Conduct does not have to be aimed at someone from the group in question for it to be harassment of that person, or of someone else in the room.** A racist comment in a Soho restaurant where two of our staff are at the table — neither of them from that ethnic group — can be harassment of those staff, full stop. We will come back to this in the microaggressions section and again in third-party. It is the single most common point of misunderstanding, and yes, that includes when nobody is from the group in question.

## Section 2: Religion or belief

The s.26 protection covers religion or any religious belief, and also any "philosophical belief" of comparable seriousness — case law has extended this to include, for example, ethical veganism and gender-critical beliefs, neither of which the firm has to agree with for the protection to apply.

The everyday failure modes are not slurs. They are mockery of religious dress, "jokes" about fasting during Ramadan, comments about prayer breaks slowing the team down, and questions about a colleague's choices around alcohol that wouldn't be asked of anyone else.

**Scenario.** A new joiner in the broker team observes Ramadan. During team lunches the manager keeps offering food in a way that becomes a running joke: "go on, one bite, we won't tell Allah." Two other colleagues join in. The joiner laughs along for the first week, then stops attending team lunches. The conduct is unwanted, related to religion, and would be considered by a reasonable person to have the effect of being humiliating or degrading. It is harassment under s.26, even if the manager believes — perhaps sincerely — that he is being playfully welcoming.

**Grey area.** A Christian colleague keeps a small crucifix on her desk and a Bible verse in her email signature. A colleague who finds religious expression in the workplace inappropriate raises it with the team lead and asks her to remove both. Is the colleague's complaint protected, or is it harassment of the Christian colleague? The honest answer: it depends on tone, frequency, what is actually said, and whether the complaint amounts to mockery of her belief or a reasonable workplace conversation about shared space. Tribunals have decided cases like this both ways. The point is not that there's a clean answer; the point is that "religion or belief" cuts in more than one direction, and the analysis is the same s.26 test applied to whoever is the recipient of the conduct.

## Section 3: Age

Age is the protected characteristic this audience dismisses fastest. "We're a young firm" is a sentence that has launched several uplifted compensation awards.

Age harassment runs in both directions. Comments about older colleagues being "behind the times," "near retirement," or "not really getting digital" are squarely in scope. So are comments about younger colleagues being "too junior to be in this room," "still wet behind the ears" when used persistently, or being assumed to be the most junior person at the table when they are not. The protection applies at every age, not just to older workers.

**Scenario.** A 56-year-old underwriter returns from sabbatical to find his team has restructured. In the first all-hands, two senior colleagues make running jokes about him needing "the IT department on speed-dial" and refer to him repeatedly as "the dinosaur." He laughs. Three weeks later he raises it with HR. The fact he laughed in the moment does not end the analysis — the s.26(4) reasonableness test asks how a reasonable person would view the cumulative effect, not how the recipient managed it in real time.

**Grey area.** A graduate-scheme intake of eight 22-year-olds sit together at every team lunch and rarely speak to anyone over 35. Older colleagues feel iced out. Is this age harassment of the older colleagues? Probably not — exclusion from a social cluster, without conduct directed at age, is unlikely to meet the s.26 test. But it is a culture problem the firm needs to address through other means (and the line between "they keep to themselves" and "they make jokes about the boomers" can be crossed quickly).

## Section 4: Gender reassignment

The protection covers anyone who is proposing to undergo, is undergoing, or has undergone a process of reassigning their sex. The person does not need to have a Gender Recognition Certificate to be protected, and the protection covers the whole arc — including before any medical or social steps have been taken.

The everyday failure modes are deliberate misgendering after correction, "deadnaming" (using a colleague's previous name after they have changed it), intrusive questions about anatomy or surgery, "jokes" about toilets and changing rooms, and comments about a colleague's appearance framed as concern.

**Scenario.** A colleague who has been at the firm for four years transitions and asks the team to use a new name and new pronouns. Most of the team adjusts within a couple of weeks. One senior colleague continues to use the previous name and pronouns in meetings, including in front of clients. When corrected, he says he "just can't get his head around it" and "it's a free country." The conduct is unwanted, related to gender reassignment, persistent after correction, and a reasonable person would consider it to have the effect of violating the colleague's dignity. It is harassment under s.26. His subjective inability or unwillingness to adapt is not a defence.

**Grey area.** A colleague holds a gender-critical belief — a belief that biological sex is immutable and that single-sex spaces should remain so — and expresses it carefully in policy conversations the firm convenes. Another colleague, who is trans, says they find this distressing and asks the firm to stop her speaking. Both colleagues hold protected beliefs under the Equality Act. The firm has to navigate this in a way that does not amount to harassment of either, which usually means rules about manner, time and place rather than rules about who is allowed to speak. This is genuinely contested territory in case law (Forstater being the leading authority); the point for staff is that "I find this distressing" is not, on its own, sufficient to require the other person's silence.

## Section 5: Sexual orientation

Protection covers orientation towards persons of the same sex, the opposite sex, or both — and applies whether the colleague is out at work or not.

The everyday failure modes are jokes that rely on a colleague's actual or assumed orientation, intrusive questions about partners, assumptions made aloud ("you'll bring your girlfriend?" to a male colleague the speaker has assumed is straight, or the reverse), and the persistent low-grade ribbing that treats orientation as the colleague's defining feature.

**Scenario.** A senior broker mentions in a Monday-morning team meeting that he watched a televised rugby match over the weekend and the camera kept lingering on "that gay one in the commentary box." Two colleagues laugh. A junior member of the team is gay but not out at work. The s.26 test applies to him as much as it would to a colleague who was out: the conduct relates to sexual orientation and a reasonable person would consider it to have the effect of creating a hostile environment for him. His being closeted does not lower the protection; if anything it heightens the cost.

**Grey area.** Two colleagues are openly gay and openly camp; they make jokes about each other's dating lives in the open-plan office that another colleague — also gay, but more private — finds tiring. Is this harassment of the third colleague? It depends on whether the conduct is directed at him, whether it persists after he has indicated discomfort, and whether a reasonable person would consider the cumulative effect to meet the test. The fact that the speakers and the recipient share a protected characteristic does not put the conduct outside the Act — s.26 is not a "you have to be in the out-group" rule.

## Section 6: Race

Race covers colour, nationality, and ethnic or national origins. The protection is broad.

The everyday failure modes are mimicked accents, "where are you really from" follow-ups, comments on hair, comments on names ("can I just call you something easier"), assumptions about food, assumptions about country of origin, and the entire class of "national stereotype" jokes that the speaker frames as light-hearted observation.

**Scenario.** At a client lunch in Soho, the most senior person at the table from our side makes a comment about a particular nationality's "approach to time-keeping." Two of our staff are at the table — neither is of that nationality. The comment is overheard by the table next to ours, where one diner clearly is. Three things to note: the comment is harassment of our two staff (it relates to race, it is unwanted, a reasonable person would consider it to create a hostile environment); the comment is potentially harassment of the diner at the next table once s.21 ERA 2025 commences in October 2026 (third-party); and the comment is a Conduct Rule issue for the senior person from 1 September 2026 under COCON 1.1.7FR.

**Grey area.** A team member who grew up in Glasgow finds his accent regularly the subject of impressions and jokes from London colleagues. He gives as good as he gets. Is this harassment? In legal terms, regional accent within Great Britain is not itself a protected characteristic — but national origin (Scottish, Welsh, English) is. So the test turns on whether the conduct is read as targeting his Scottishness rather than his accent per se. The honest answer is that it sits close to the line, and "he gives as good as he gets" is not an automatic defence — see the purpose-or-effect point again.

## Section 7: Disability

A disabled person under the Equality Act is someone with a physical or mental impairment with a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. Conditions covered include — among many others — diabetes, multiple sclerosis, depression, anxiety disorders, autism, ADHD, dyslexia, long Covid (where the substantial-and-long-term test is met — assessed case by case), and HIV. Cancer, HIV and MS are protected from the point of diagnosis regardless of effect.

The everyday failure modes are jokes about a colleague's stammer or tic, mimicking, mock surprise at requested adjustments, framing a reasonable adjustment as "special treatment," persistent commentary on a colleague's mental-health absences, and the cluster of language around neurodivergent colleagues ("she's being so autistic about this").

**Scenario.** A colleague with ADHD has agreed an adjustment with the firm: written agendas before meetings and tasks confirmed in writing afterwards. Her manager mocks this in the team Teams channel — "in case anyone forgets, [name] needs everything in writing because reasons" — with a thinking-emoji. The conduct is unwanted, related to disability, in writing, and a reasonable person would consider it to have the effect of being humiliating. It is harassment, and separately it likely undermines the firm's reasonable-adjustments obligation under the Equality Act.

**Grey area.** A colleague has a diagnosis of generalised anxiety disorder and has asked not to be put on client calls. He has had no formal occupational-health assessment. His manager, who has lost three other team members to long-term absence, is sceptical and asks him to "give it a go." Is the manager's pushback harassment, a reasonable management instruction, or a failure to make reasonable adjustments? It depends on tone, persistence, and how the conversation is handled. The protective move is to route the question through HR and occupational health, document the analysis, and decide on that basis. Doing it informally in a corridor, repeatedly, is where managers walk into trouble.

## Section 8: Sex

Sex covers being male or being female. The protection applies in both directions, though the substantial majority of reported cases involve conduct towards women.

Sexual harassment is a related-but-distinct category covered fully in Module 1 (§4) and revisited in Module 4 (power dynamics). This section is about harassment **related to** sex that is not necessarily sexual in nature — comments about competence based on sex, assumptions about caring responsibilities, language that treats one sex as the default and the other as the exception, and the cumulative low-level patterns that women in finance report year after year.

**Scenario.** A female associate returns from maternity leave to find that the high-profile deal she had been promised has been quietly given to a male colleague at her grade. When she raises it, her managing director tells her it's "for her own good given the new circumstances," and that she should "take a few months to settle back in." Two readings: this is potentially sex discrimination (direct, under s.13) and pregnancy/maternity discrimination (under s.18), and depending on what is said in subsequent conversations it may also be harassment related to sex under s.26. The "for her own good" framing is not a defence — it is, in evidential terms, often what gets the case won.

**Grey area.** A male colleague raises that he feels excluded from a women-in-finance networking group that meets monthly inside the firm. Is the existence of the group, or its restriction to female colleagues, harassment of him? Almost certainly not — the Equality Act permits positive action and the existence of an affinity group is not conduct directed at him. But the question is fair, and the answer matters: protective measures aimed at one group are not, in themselves, harassment of the other.

## Section 9: Marriage / civil partnership, and pregnancy / maternity

These two characteristics sit outside s.26 — you cannot bring a harassment claim under the Equality Act on the basis of marriage or pregnancy alone.

That is the legal position. It is not a permission slip. Less favourable treatment on either ground is **direct discrimination** under s.13 or s.18 EA 2010 and is fully actionable. The "for her own good" maternity scenario above is the everyday example. Comments and conduct related to pregnancy or maternity that meet the s.26 test may also be harassment **related to sex**, since pregnancy and maternity are, in tribunal practice, often analysed through the sex-related lens where the conduct is gendered.

The practical instruction: do not treat the omission of these two from s.26 as a gap you can drive a truck through. Direct discrimination claims, with their own remedies and reputational consequences, sit immediately behind it.

## Section 10: Bullying vs harassment

ACAS draws the line cleanly. **Bullying** is offensive, intimidating, malicious or insulting behaviour — but bullying, as such, has no specific statute in UK employment law. **Harassment** is bullying that is **related to a protected characteristic**, which makes it actionable under the Equality Act.

In practical terms, most workers will use the word "bullying" when they describe what is in fact harassment. Part of a manager's job is to listen carefully enough to know which one they are hearing. A "bullying" report from a colleague about a manager who consistently shouts at the women on the team but never at the men is a harassment report, not a bullying one. A "bullying" report about a manager who shouts at everyone equally is a serious cultural problem and possibly a constructive-dismissal claim — but not a s.26 harassment claim.

Bullying without a protected-characteristic dimension is still a sackable offence and is still actionable in some routes (constructive dismissal; from 1 September 2026, COCON 1.1.7FR for FCA-regulated firms). It does not require the EA 2010 framework to be a problem. But the legal lever is different, and the manager who can tell which one they are dealing with is the manager who handles the report properly.

## Section 11: Microaggressions and the cumulative-effect point

A single low-level comment will rarely meet the s.26 test on its own. Twenty of them, from different sources, over six months, very often will — because s.26 looks at the **environment**, and an environment is cumulative.

This is the point that resists training. Each individual comment is "not a big deal," and the speaker can be sincere in saying so. The recipient is asked to assess each one against a threshold none of them would individually meet, and the result is that nothing is ever raised until something major happens and a long quiet pattern surfaces all at once.

Four examples — one from each of four characteristics — to anchor the point.

- **Age.** "You're so good with computers, considering" — directed at a colleague in their late 50s for the third time in a fortnight.
- **Race.** "Where are you really from?" — and the variants, asked of a colleague whose accent is identical to the asker's.
- **Gender reassignment.** "You used to be a man, right?" — asked in passing in front of others by someone who knows the answer.
- **Religion or belief.** "Are you sure you don't want a drink? It's just a glass" — to the same Muslim colleague, every Friday, for six months.

Each line, in isolation, looks survivable. The colleague survives each one and then resigns. The exit interview is the first time the firm hears the cumulative reading. That is, in evidential terms, exactly the pattern tribunals and the EHRC are most familiar with — and exactly the pattern they look for as a sign that the firm's culture did not catch it earlier.

## Section 12: Third parties — clients, contractors, visitors

From **1 October 2026**, s.21 of the Employment Rights Act 2025 makes the firm directly liable for harassment of its employees by **third parties** — clients, customers, contractors, suppliers, visitors, anyone the firm's people deal with through the job. The new s.40(1A) EA 2010 will read that the firm "must not permit" such harassment, with permission defined by failure to take all reasonable steps.

This applies to all seven harassment-relevant characteristics, not only to sex.

**Scenario 1.** A client makes a comment about a member of the reception team's appearance during a visit, then asks her for her personal number. She is uncomfortable; she tells her line manager that afternoon. From October 2026, the firm's response — whether it speaks to the client, whether it briefs reception staff to handle future visits, whether it documents the decision — is directly relevant to whether it has taken all reasonable steps. "He's a major client" is not, on its own, a defence.

**Scenario 2.** A contractor placed on a deal team for six weeks makes a running set of jokes about a junior analyst's accent. The analyst raises it with the deal lead, who tells her the contractor "is just like that" and to keep her head down for the remaining four weeks. The firm is now in the position it would be in if the harasser were an employee, with one twist: the contractor is not in the disciplinary system, so the firm's lever is the contract under which they are engaged. Doing nothing is the worst option available, including from a regulatory standpoint.

The shift from October 2026 is not subtle. Customer-facing teams in particular need a documented escalation route, and senior people need to know they have authority to push back on a client whose behaviour is creating a hostile environment for staff — including by withdrawing service if it comes to that.

## Section 13: What to take from this module

- The Equality Act protects against harassment related to seven characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation. Marriage/civil partnership and pregnancy/maternity sit outside s.26 but are protected against direct discrimination — and gendered conduct around pregnancy is often analysable as harassment related to sex.
- Conduct does not have to be aimed at someone from the group in question. A comment about Group X in a room where nobody from Group X is present can still be harassment of the people who heard it.
- "It was just banter," "I didn't mean it," and "she laughed it off" are not defences. Section 26(4) asks what a reasonable person would conclude about the effect.
- Bullying and harassment are not the same thing legally. Listen carefully enough to know which one a colleague is describing — the legal route is different, but both can end someone's career.
- Microaggressions accumulate. The threshold is environmental, not per-incident, and the firm rarely hears the cumulative pattern until it is too late.
- From 1 October 2026, the firm is directly liable for third-party harassment across all seven characteristics. "He's a major client" is not a defence.
- For FCA-regulated colleagues, a substantiated harassment finding from 1 September 2026 is also a fitness-and-propriety question, not only an HR one.

## Reflection prompts

Two open-text prompts. **Logged but not scored. Your line manager will never see your answers** — only Group HR, and only as anonymised aggregate themes.

1. Of the seven characteristics covered above, which one feels least intuitive to you — the one where, if you're honest, you'd struggle to spot harassment in the moment because the patterns are unfamiliar? You don't have to name colleagues; just the characteristic and what you think the gap is.

2. Think of a piece of conduct you have seen at work in the last twelve months that, on the cumulative-effect reading from Section 11, you'd now read differently than you did at the time. What would you do differently next time — and what (if anything) would stop you doing it?
