# Module 1 — The Law and Why It Matters

**Duration:** ~12 minutes reading + 8-question test

## Section 1: Why this training exists

UK law has changed. As of October 2024, every employer has a legal duty to take **reasonable steps** to prevent sexual harassment at work. From October 2026, that duty rises to taking **all reasonable steps**, and it now covers harassment by third parties — clients, auditors, contractors, anyone we deal with — not just colleagues.

This isn't just policy. An employment tribunal can uplift compensation by up to 25% if we haven't met the duty, and the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) can take enforcement action against the firm directly. In financial services, there's an additional layer: the FCA now treats non-financial misconduct — including harassment, bullying, and discrimination — as relevant to a person's fitness and propriety under the Senior Managers and Certification Regime. A serious finding can affect your regulatory status, not just your employment.

The aim of this module is to give you the legal foundation. The later modules cover what harassment looks like in practice, what to do if you see it, and how to report it.

## Section 2: The Equality Act 2010 — the foundation

The Equality Act 2010 protects people from discrimination based on nine **protected characteristics**:

- Age
- Disability
- Gender reassignment
- Marriage and civil partnership
- Pregnancy and maternity
- Race
- Religion or belief
- Sex
- Sexual orientation

The Act recognises four main types of prohibited conduct: direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, and victimisation. This training focuses on harassment, but the others matter too — for example, treating someone less favourably because they are pregnant is direct discrimination, and punishing someone for raising a harassment complaint is victimisation.

One technical point that frequently catches people out. Of the nine protected characteristics, only **seven** trigger the s.26 harassment provisions — marriage/civil partnership and pregnancy/maternity sit outside s.26 and are instead protected against direct discrimination under s.13 and s.18. So when we say "harassment-relevant characteristics" later, we mean: age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation. Module 2 covers each of those in detail.

## Section 3: What harassment legally means

The Equality Act defines harassment as **unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic** which has the **purpose or effect** of violating someone's dignity, or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment for them.

Three points to absorb carefully, because they're frequently misunderstood.

**"Purpose or effect."** It does not matter whether the person doing it intended to cause offence. If a reasonable person would consider the conduct had that effect on the recipient, it can be harassment. "It was just banter" and "I didn't mean it that way" are not defences.

**"Related to" a protected characteristic.** The conduct doesn't have to be directed at the person who has the characteristic. A racist joke told in front of a colleague of any race can be harassment of that colleague. The same applies to comments about a third party — for example, derogatory remarks about a client's religion made within earshot of staff.

**Single incidents count.** Harassment is not just about repeated behaviour. One sufficiently serious incident — an unwanted physical contact, a single explicit comment — can meet the legal definition on its own.

## Section 4: Sexual harassment specifically

Sexual harassment is treated as its own category. It is **unwanted conduct of a sexual nature** with the same purpose-or-effect test. Examples include sexual comments, jokes or innuendo, unwanted physical contact, displaying or sharing sexual images, persistent requests for dates after refusal, and inappropriate questions about someone's sex life or relationships.

There is also **less favourable treatment for rejecting or submitting to** sexual conduct — for example, a manager who passes someone over for a project after they declined a date. This is harassment under the Act even if the underlying conduct alone wouldn't qualify.

Sexual harassment can occur between any combination of people regardless of gender. The law does not require the harasser and the recipient to be of different sexes.

## Section 5: The Worker Protection Act 2023 and the 2026 uplift

Before October 2024, an employer was only liable for harassment by employees after the fact, with a defence available if they had taken reasonable steps to prevent it. The Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023 changed this by adding a **proactive duty**: employers must take reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment before it happens.

From October 2026, the Employment Rights Act 2025 raises this in two ways:

1. The standard becomes **all reasonable steps** — a deliberately higher bar.
2. The duty extends to harassment by **third parties**: clients, contractors, suppliers, visitors, and anyone else workers encounter through their job.

Tribunals will look not just at whether policies exist, but at how they're implemented — including the frequency and quality of training, the accessibility of reporting routes, and whether risk assessments have actually been done.

## Section 6: Financial services context

The FCA expects regulated firms to maintain healthy cultures. Non-financial misconduct — harassment, bullying, discrimination — is treated as relevant conduct for SMCR purposes. From 1 September 2026, COCON 1.1.7FR extends the Conduct Rules to harassment, bullying and violence against colleagues in every SMCR-regulated firm — not just banks. A substantiated finding against a Senior Manager or Certified person can affect their certification; the firm has reporting obligations in serious cases; and the new SYSC 22 regulatory-reference rule means a substantiated finding follows the individual when they move firm.

The whistleblowing protections that took effect on 6 April 2026 mean that any disclosure relating to sexual harassment automatically qualifies as a protected disclosure under whistleblowing law.

In practical terms: harassment is no longer just an HR issue in finance. It's a regulatory issue.

## Section 7: What you need to take from this module

- Harassment is unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic, judged by its effect, not the intent.
- There are nine protected characteristics, not just sex.
- Sexual harassment is its own category and includes a wide range of conduct, not just physical contact.
- The employer has a proactive duty to prevent harassment, rising to "all reasonable steps" in October 2026, including from third parties.
- In financial services, non-financial misconduct affects regulatory status under SMCR — and from 1 September 2026, that applies to every regulated firm.
- Retaliation against someone who reports harassment is itself unlawful, and from 6 April 2026 a sexual-harassment disclosure can be a protected whistleblowing disclosure with uncapped damages on retaliation.
