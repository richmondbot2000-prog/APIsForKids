## Section 1: Power and the law

Harassment law cares about effect, not intent. Power dynamics decide which effects are easy to produce and which are easy to deny. This module is about the situations where the gap between what someone can do and what someone else can refuse is wide enough that ordinary social rules stop protecting people.

The most direct statutory hook sits in **section 26(3) of the Equality Act 2010**. It treats as harassment any **less favourable treatment** of a person because they **rejected or submitted to** unwanted conduct of a sexual nature, or other conduct related to sex or gender reassignment. The conduct that triggered the rejection or submission does not itself have to be unlawful. The "less favourable treatment" — being passed over for the mandate, the project, the bonus pool, the next round — is what the law catches.

The worked example from Module 1 is the simplest illustration: a manager who passes a team member over for a high-profile project shortly after that team member declined a dinner invitation. The Equality Act treats the project decision and the prior invitation as connected — and the burden of proof effectively shifts to the manager to show they weren't.

Expanded into how this actually shows up in finance:

- The mandate that "doesn't quite suit" the analyst who pulled out of the off-site dinner.
- The mid-year review that was strong last cycle and is suddenly "developmental" after a date was declined.
- The desk transfer "for development reasons" that follows a private conversation in which a junior said no.
- The introduction to a client that quietly stops happening after a comment was rebuffed.

**Power does not have to be formal.** Section 26(3) applies whether or not there is a reporting line. Peer relationships, mentor relationships, client-side relationships and certified-person relationships (where one party signs off on another's competence or attestation) all create the asymmetry the section was written for. A senior trader who controls allocation, a partner who controls origination credit, a certifier who controls F&P attestation, a client who controls retainer renewal — these are all power relationships even where no org chart connects them.

The remainder of this module names the situations in which that asymmetry most often does damage.

## Section 2: The high-risk situations, finance-specific

### 2.1 Bonus and appraisal context

The most leveraged conversation in finance is the conversation about future earning potential. It is also the conversation most often used as a vector for inappropriate conduct, because the framing is plausibly benign — "I'm trying to help your career" — and the cost of the listener pushing back is unusually concrete.

The technical legal name is **quid pro quo harassment**. The Equality Act doesn't use that phrase, but section 26(3) catches it. The two ends of the spectrum:

- **Explicit:** "I could really put you forward for that big mandate if we got to know each other better." This is the version everyone recognises in the abstract and almost no-one says exactly. When it does happen it tends to come at the end of a long evening, in a one-on-one, with no witnesses.
- **Implicit:** the comment about "future earning potential" that floats next to a suggestion of dinner, a drink, a weekend invitation. The implicit version is the one that does most of the damage in practice — because it is plausibly deniable, because the recipient is left wondering whether they heard what they heard, and because the next bonus or appraisal conversation is now contaminated regardless of what either party meant.

In an SMCR firm, this is also a specific regulatory exposure. **Remuneration is a regulated decision in many SMCR firms** — the SYSC remuneration regime treats bonus determination as a controlled process. A senior person who introduces personal-interest considerations into that process puts the firm's compliance with the remuneration code into question, as well as their own fitness and propriety.

If you are the recipient of one of these comments:

- Document the conversation the same day. Notes app, email-to-self, with date, time, location and direct quote where possible. (Module 3 §6.)
- If there is a witness, take a beat with them afterwards — not to gossip, to anchor the recollection.
- Escalate through a route that does not run through the person who made the comment. Most firms have a Speak Up channel for exactly this reason.

If you are senior and you are about to have a conversation that touches earning potential: be sober, be on the firm's premises or its scheduled venue, be in business hours where possible, and stay within the structured competency vocabulary the appraisal system gives you. The off-piste version of the conversation is the version that creates risk for everyone in it.

### 2.2 Client entertainment and client dinners

The classic shape: a senior client of any gender, a junior banker / broker / underwriter / relationship-manager, a long dinner, a wine list that runs longer than the agenda. The point at which the senior client stops behaving like a client and starts behaving like someone using the dinner as cover is rarely a single line — it is a drift across a series of small thresholds.

The pre-October 2026 firm position, in many corners of finance, was that "the client behaved badly but they're not our employee" amounted to a partial defence. **From 1 October 2026, section 40(1A)–(1C) of the Equality Act 2010 (inserted by section 21 of the Employment Rights Act 2025) makes the firm directly liable for harassment of its employees by clients, contractors, suppliers, visitors and any other third party.** The defence is now "all reasonable steps." Doing nothing about a known problem client — or about the dinner culture that produces problem moments — will not survive a tribunal that asks the question.

What this means in practice:

- Junior staff should never be alone with senior clients at venues serving alcohol unless there is a real, named reason and an escalation route that doesn't depend on them. "Junior gets the dinner because senior had a conflict" is not a real reason.
- Where a client behaves inappropriately, the firm's response — not the affected employee's response — is what the law looks at. The firm has an institutional duty to address it, including being prepared to refuse to staff that client with that employee again, or refuse the client at all.
- "Important client" is not a reason to dilute the response. It is a reason to be more rigorous about it, because the institutional incentive is to look the other way.

If you are the affected employee: leave the venue early without apology, document the same evening, and escalate to your line manager or to HR the following morning. The firm cannot meet its duty if it does not know.

### 2.3 Off-sites and away-days

The structural problem with off-sites is that the agenda ends and the hotel bar doesn't. The structural problem with off-site harassment is that everyone present has the same incentive to call it "we were all having a laugh" the next morning.

That excuse never landed in law. **From 1 October 2026 the firm's exposure on its own off-sites is direct.** The off-site is the firm's event, on the firm's time, with attendance effectively required — every employment-law analysis treats it as squarely within course-of-employment. The "all reasonable steps" duty applies whether the agenda has finished or not.

What the firm should be doing — and what you, as an attendee, can expect:

- A written code of conduct circulated in advance, naming alcohol limits, room invitations, and the escalation route.
- A named, sober point of contact each evening, not just the COO who is also at the bar.
- No retaliation, ever, against an employee who leaves an off-site early because something happened.

What you can do as a participant:

- If you see something at the bar, the 5Ds from Module 3 apply — Delegate to the most senior person not in the exchange; Document; Delay-check on the affected colleague the following morning.
- If something happens to you, leave when you need to leave. The off-site does not have the authority to require you to stay in a room you don't want to be in.
- Don't trust the next-morning version of what happened. The version captured the same night, in writing, by the people who saw it, is the version that survives.

### 2.4 Work travel

Same flight, same hotel, same conference. A manager and a junior. The knock on the door at 11pm. This is among the most consistently surfaced pattern in finance-sector harassment investigations and there is no folk wisdom that makes it benign.

Course-of-employment applies. The firm's duty applies. The fact that the conduct occurred in a hotel room rather than an office does not exempt the firm or the individual.

The structural protection the firm should provide — and that you can expect:

- Separate floors where the booking allows; if not, separate corridors.
- No 1:1 work-travel pairings of a manager and a single direct report where a small adjustment to the trip would avoid it.
- A clear "I'm switching hotels at the firm's expense" route, with no questions asked, for any employee who needs it.

The personal protection: if you are senior and travelling with a junior direct report, your job is to make the trip professionally indistinguishable from a trip with five direct reports. Drinks end early. The dinner conversation is the same conversation you would have in front of HR. There is no "I just wanted to check you were OK" knock on a hotel-room door at 11pm.

### 2.5 Deal-closing and completion drinks

Big-deal-just-closed energy is its own risk category. The mortgage that completed at 6pm. The deal that signed at midnight. The mandate that just won. The endorphin baseline is high, the team is in a venue together, and lines that would never normally be crossed get crossed because the day felt special.

The law does not care that the day felt special. The Equality Act applies; the firm's preventative duty applies; the same conduct that would be a Conduct Rule breach on a Tuesday afternoon is a Conduct Rule breach at midnight on completion day.

If you are leading a deal team, you set the tone. Make it explicit on the way to the venue: we celebrate, we do not lose the plot. If you are a junior on a deal team: you have full permission to leave when you want to leave, and the firm's culture, by policy, treats early departure from completion drinks as normal.

### 2.6 Consensual workplace relationships and the disclosure expectation

The firm's policy — see Policy HR-12, Workplace Relationships — is that relationships between colleagues must be disclosed where any of the following applies:

- A reporting line, direct or indirect, exists between the two people.
- One party is in an SMCR Senior Manager or Certified role and the other is in the same regulated business.
- One party performs a certification, attestation, supervisory or audit function over the other's work.
- Any other material power asymmetry exists — for example, mentor / mentee in a structured programme, allocation of work, sign-off on bonus or appraisal.

Disclosure is not surveillance. The firm is not interested in who is sleeping with whom in the abstract. The firm is interested in two specific things:

1. **Removing the conflict.** Where a reporting or sign-off relationship exists, one of the two people will be re-assigned, so neither has decision-making authority over the other's compensation, performance review, certification or career.
2. **Protecting both parties if the relationship ends.** The post-breakup risk is real and the firm's experience is that it surfaces most often in two patterns: continued unwanted contact through work channels (the Module 1 Q13 scenario — Teams DMs after a relationship ends), and a sudden change in tone of performance feedback from one party who still has authority over the other. Disclosure, made earlier, lets the firm prevent both.

What disclosure looks like, in practice: a short conversation with HR — the substance, not the colour. The conversation is confidential. It does not appear in your appraisal. It does not appear to your line manager unless a re-assignment is needed, in which case the conversation will be had.

What it is not: a public statement, a moral judgment, or an invitation for the firm to manage the relationship. The boundary between disclosure and surveillance is the boundary between "we know there is a structural issue and have addressed it" and "we are interested in your private life," and the firm sits firmly on the disclosure side.

The cost of not disclosing — and the post-breakup risk in particular — is that conduct that would have been managed quietly becomes a Conduct Rule matter, a victimisation claim under s.27 EA 2010, or a protected-disclosure case under the April 2026 whistleblowing reforms. Disclosure is the cheap option for everyone involved.

### 2.7 Third-party harassment — a brief introduction

From 1 October 2026 the firm is directly liable for harassment of its employees by third parties — clients, contractors, suppliers, visitors, anyone the employee encounters in the course of employment — across all protected characteristics, not just sex. The defence is "all reasonable steps."

Module 5 covers this in detail. The point to absorb here is that the third-party regime closes the historical exit hatch in which a firm treated client-side or contractor conduct as outside its control. From October 2026 it isn't.

## Section 3: What managers must do differently

Module M (the Manager add-on) covers this in depth. Two principles to absorb now, because they explain a category of manager behaviour that everyone — not just managers — needs to be able to recognise.

**You cannot be the harasser and also the recipient's complaint route.** This is structural, not personal. Even if your team genuinely believes you would handle a complaint fairly, the structure of asking someone to raise a concern with the person it is about does not produce reliable disclosure. The firm's reporting routes deliberately include channels that bypass the line manager — Speak Up, HR direct, the named senior owner of the issue, the audit committee — because the literature and the FCA both treat single-channel reporting as evidence that the system isn't working. If you are a manager: do not be defensive about the bypass. It exists because the structure requires it, not because anyone doubts you.

**Performance management as retaliation is the canary.** When a complaint is filed and the complainant's next appraisal is suddenly weaker than the prior one — or a new performance concern surfaces that wasn't there last month, or work is withdrawn, or a development plan appears — the pattern fits a textbook s.27 EA 2010 victimisation claim. The Manager add-on covers in depth how to handle appraisal of a complainant; the point here, for non-managers, is that you should recognise this pattern when you see it happening to a colleague. It is the single most reliable external signal that something has gone wrong in the response to a complaint.

The Manager add-on covers receiving disclosures as a named skill, the investigation duty, evidence preservation, the no-weaponising-performance-management rule, the duty to act without a formal complaint, and the third-party regime. It is a hard requirement on appointment as a line manager and for every SMCR-certified individual.

## Section 4: The FCA / SMCR overlay

A senior person abusing power asymmetry is not only an Equality Act matter. From **1 September 2026, COCON 1.1.7FR** extends the Conduct Rules to bullying, harassment and violence against colleagues across all SMCR firms. The seriousness threshold is the Equality Act test verbatim. Approximately 37,000 additional firms come into scope.

In practical terms: a Senior Manager or certified person who behaves in the ways this module describes — the bonus comment, the late knock on the hotel-room door, the withdrawal of the project after the date was declined — is exposed to **Individual Conduct Rule 1 (integrity)**. A substantiated finding is directly relevant to their **fitness and propriety**, and from 1 September 2026 must be disclosed in regulatory references when they move firm (SYSC 22). The "pass the harasser" route is closed.

The **Odey precedent** (3 March 2025) is the public marker that the FCA will use these powers at the most senior level — a £1.8m fine and an industry ban under ICR 1, for conduct around the handling of sexual-harassment allegations. The case is currently under Upper Tribunal review but the Decision Notice is the proof of regulatory appetite. The point is not the size of the fine. The point is that the FCA treated the conduct around the matter — the framing of the response, the handling of the allegations — as a regulated integrity issue. That is the bar against which behaviour in the situations in this module will now be measured.

Sarah Pritchard, FCA Deputy Chief Executive (2 July 2025), put it as cleanly as the regulator will:

> "Behaviour like bullying or harassment going unchallenged is one of the reddest flags — a culture where this occurs can raise questions about a firm's decision making and risk management."

If you are SMCR-certified and reading this module, the regulator's interest is not theoretical. The April 2026 whistleblowing reform (s.23 ERA 2025) means any qualifying disclosure relating to sexual harassment is a protected disclosure under the whistleblowing regime, with uncapped damages for detriment in response. The combination of COCON 1.1.7FR, FIT 2.1, SYSC 22 regulatory references and s.27 EA 2010 victimisation protection means there is no safe corner from which a senior person can mishandle one of these situations and expect the regulator not to take an interest.

## Section 5: What to take from this module

- Power asymmetry is what most of harassment law is built around. Section 26(3) of the Equality Act 2010 catches less favourable treatment for rejecting or submitting to sexual conduct, with or without a formal reporting line.
- The **bonus and appraisal conversation** is the most leveraged conversation in finance and the most commonly used vector for quid-pro-quo conduct, including in its implicit, plausibly-deniable form. Remuneration is also a regulated decision in SMCR firms.
- **Client entertainment, off-sites, work travel, and deal-closing drinks** are the four contexts where the firm's preventative duty most often fails. From 1 October 2026 the "client / contractor / visitor isn't our employee" defence is gone — the firm is directly liable for third-party harassment.
- **Consensual workplace relationships must be disclosed** where a reporting line, SMCR / certified relationship, or any material power asymmetry exists. Disclosure is not surveillance — it lets the firm remove the conflict and protect both parties if the relationship ends.
- **Performance management as retaliation** — adverse appraisal, withdrawn work, sudden development plan — is the textbook s.27 EA 2010 victimisation pattern and the most reliable external signal that something has gone wrong in the response to a complaint.
- A senior person mishandling these situations is exposed to **Conduct Rule 1 (integrity)** from 1 September 2026 under COCON 1.1.7FR, with consequences for fitness and propriety, regulatory references and — in serious cases — prohibition. The Odey precedent is the public marker.
- The April 2026 whistleblowing reforms (s.23 ERA 2025) and s.27 EA 2010 victimisation protection mean retaliating against anyone who raises a concern carries uncapped damages and separate regulatory exposure of its own.
