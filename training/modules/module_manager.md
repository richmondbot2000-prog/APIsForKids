# Module M — Manager add-on

**Audience:** Anyone whose Line-Manager-of field on the TogetherBook Directory is non-empty, plus anyone SMCR-certified.
**Duration:** ~20 minutes reading + 10-question test
**Deadline:** 14 days from appointment to a line-manager role or to a certified function.

---

## Section 1: Why this module exists separately

Module 5 covers what a person raising a concern needs to know. This module covers what a manager hearing one is required to do — including, more importantly, what a manager must not do. The two are different competencies and the law treats them differently.

The EEOC Select Task Force on Harassment (Feldblum & Lipnic, 2016) — the most authoritative regulator review of training effectiveness — singles out the manager track as the single highest-leverage component of a prevention programme. Managers face role-specific dilemmas that simply don't appear in all-staff content: handling a disclosure, intervening across hierarchy, deciding whether to act in the absence of a written complaint, managing a team during an investigation, and making the everyday performance decisions that, badly handled, become victimisation claims.

From **1 September 2026**, the FCA's new Handbook rule **COCON 1.1.7FR** makes the manager's own handling of non-financial misconduct a regulated matter for all SMCR firms. Mishandling a disclosure can itself be the Conduct Rule breach — separate from any breach by the alleged perpetrator. That is new. It is also why this module is non-optional for any line manager or SMCR-certified person, with a 14-day deadline from appointment.

A manager who completes Modules 1-5 has the all-staff foundation. This module is the additional layer that sits on top.

## Section 2: Receiving a disclosure — the named skill

Receiving a disclosure is a skill in its own right, not a casual conversation. LSE treats it as a discrete manager competency; so does the EHRC under Step 6 of the Eight Steps. The mechanics matter because the way you handle the first conversation shapes everything that follows.

### What to do

**Listen, and don't interrupt.** The person in front of you has often rehearsed this conversation for weeks. Let them finish before you start asking questions. Long silences are fine.

**Thank them for raising it.** Not effusively; plainly. They have done the hard part.

**Take contemporaneous notes.** Write down what they said, not what you think they meant. Use their words where you can. Timestamp the note. The most defensible format is a notes-app entry to yourself, dated, that you don't subsequently edit — see Section 4 on evidence preservation.

**Tell them what happens next.** Specifically: who you will escalate to (HR, Speak Up, your own line manager, the named EHRC-step-4 contact for your business area), when you will do it (typically same day or next working day), and what they should expect to hear back. Vagueness here is corrosive — they will fill the silence with the worst-case version.

**Ask what they want to happen.** Not as a veto over your duty (see Section 3 below), but because their preferences are a strong factor in how the matter proceeds and you need to know them.

**Offer the routes in Module 5 §6.** Skip-level, HR, Speak Up, EHRC, ACAS, police. Not to push them away from you — to make clear they have options.

### What NOT to do

**Don't promise outcomes.** "I'll make sure they're disciplined" / "I'll keep you safe" / "I'll handle this personally" — none of these are yours to promise. You can promise that you will take it seriously and that you will act; you cannot promise what the investigation will find or what sanction will follow.

**Don't promise absolute confidentiality.** You can't keep that promise. See Module 5 §2 for the honest version of what the firm can promise.

**Don't investigate yourself.** This is the most common manager mistake. The fact that the disclosure came to you does not make you the investigator. Stand down. The matter goes to HR or to a named investigator who is not in either party's reporting line.

**Don't decide whether the complaint is "valid."** Your job at the receiving stage is to hear it and to escalate it. Adjudication is for the investigator and the hearing manager.

**Don't tell them to "see if it stops" or "give it a chance to settle."** This is the line that ends up as Exhibit 1 in the tribunal bundle. It also breaches the manager's own COCON 1.1.7FR duty as of 1 September 2026.

**Don't discuss the matter with peers.** Not at lunch, not in the corridor, not in a "between you and me." Each onward conversation is a confidentiality breach that the complainant can document.

**Don't tell the alleged perpetrator before HR has had the chance to plan the conversation.** A pre-warning destroys evidence (deleted messages, coached witnesses, rehearsed answers) and converts a normal investigation into a much harder one.

**Don't write the disclosure up in a way that would damage the complainant if it leaked.** Stick to facts. Avoid editorialising. The notes you take may end up in front of the complainant, the respondent, an investigator, a tribunal, and the FCA. Write them in that knowledge.

### The "but they don't want a formal complaint" trap

This is the dilemma every manager will hit. The complainant tells you what happened and then says: *please don't escalate this, I just wanted you to know, I don't want a formal complaint.*

The honest position is more complicated than either "you must escalate" or "you must respect their wishes."

**You have an independent legal duty to act on what you now know.** That duty doesn't depend on a paper trail or on the complainant's consent. It flows from the s.40A Equality Act 2010 preventative duty (the firm must take reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment) and, from 1 September 2026, from COCON 1.1.7FR (your own Conduct Rule obligation). A manager who hears a disclosure and does nothing has potentially breached both — independent of anything the alleged perpetrator did.

**The complainant's wishes are a very strong factor in *how* you act.** Not whether. How. The fact that they don't want a formal investigation may legitimately shape whether you go straight to a written grievance or whether HR runs a quieter parallel risk-assessment first; it may shape whether you speak to the alleged perpetrator directly or arrange for someone else to; it may shape what is recorded and where. It does not give you permission to do nothing.

**Equally — you don't have permission to escalate against the complainant's express wishes without a serious reason.** Going behind their back, naming them in a formal complaint they didn't authorise, or triggering a process they explicitly asked you not to trigger, is itself a manager-side mistake. It often makes the complainant the second victim of the process and damages trust in the system for everyone else watching.

**The right move is to consult HR confidentially about the dilemma.** Frame it as a question, not as a request for permission: *I have heard a disclosure. The complainant is reluctant to escalate. I have an independent duty to act. What is the right shape of action that respects their wishes as far as I can?* HR will help you find the shape — an informal risk-assessment, a heightened watch on the alleged perpetrator's other interactions, a one-on-one with the alleged perpetrator framed around behaviour generally rather than the specific allegation, a pattern-record entry against a name without a formal proceeding attached. The exact answer depends on the matter; the principle is that consultation is the discharge of your duty, silence is not.

## Section 3: The duty to act without a formal complaint

The dilemma in Section 2 sits inside a broader principle that managers often miss.

**Your duty to act does not depend on someone filing.** You witnessed something. You overheard something. A team member mentioned something in passing and changed the subject. Nobody has put pen to paper.

The s.40A preventative duty is not triggered by a complaint. It is a free-standing, anticipatory duty. The EHRC Step 3 risk-assessment requirement says explicitly: *if you have identified a risk, you must act on it.* Awareness, not paperwork, is the trigger. The Bird & Bird "one year of the preventative duty" review of post-October-2024 tribunal practice flags this as one of the most common employer failures — managers who knew, didn't act, and then tried to argue the duty was paperwork-dependent.

From 1 September 2026, the same principle is embedded in your individual COCON 1.1.7FR obligation. A manager who heard about a problem and didn't escalate it is in scope for a personal Conduct Rule investigation, regardless of whether anyone else complained.

### Worked example — the off-site bar

You're at an industry off-site. Late in the evening, in the bar, you overhear a senior colleague making a comment to a junior member of another team that crosses the line — specifically, a sexual comment about her appearance dressed up as a compliment. She visibly recoils. He laughs and turns away.

You are not her manager. She has not asked you for help. Nobody has complained.

Two routes, neither of which is optional:

**Route 1 — direct intervention in the moment (5Ds framework, Module 3).** "Direct" — *that comment wasn't OK, please don't do that.* Or "Delay" — approaching her later, *I saw what he said earlier, are you OK, can I help with anything from here.* The 5Ds are designed precisely for this kind of moment and they do not require any formal authority.

**Route 2 — a confidential note to HR for the pattern record.** Even if she does not want to take it forward, the firm needs to know. The alleged perpetrator has now been observed by a manager doing something that crosses the line. If a similar pattern emerges later, the contemporaneous note is the difference between *we had no idea* and *we had observed this and were monitoring it*. The note should be timestamped, factual, and sent to a named HR contact.

The manager who walks back to their hotel room and does nothing is in breach of the s.40A duty and (from 1 September 2026) of their own COCON 1.1.7FR obligation. *I didn't want to make it awkward* is not a defence.

## Section 4: Evidence preservation

Once you're aware of a matter, you are part of the evidential chain whether you want to be or not. A few rules.

**Contemporaneous notes.** Write the note when the event happens, not later. The most defensible format is a notes-app entry to yourself, timestamped, that you don't subsequently edit. An edited Word document is materially less defensible than an unedited notes-app entry because the metadata of a Word file shows when it was changed and by whom. If you must use Word, save sequential versions rather than overwriting.

**Preserve the digital trail.** Teams chats, emails, calendar invitations relevant to the period are evidence. Do not delete anything. If you are tempted to "tidy up" a Teams channel or a chat thread that contains relevant material, stop — that's potentially destruction of evidence. The firm's IT retention policy already preserves most of it server-side, but your local actions can still create an inference.

**Do not access the alleged perpetrator's email or messages yourself.** This is not your job and it creates a serious chain-of-custody problem. Forensic IT or HR will do it through the proper channel with the proper authorisation. A manager who logs in to a colleague's account, even with the best intentions, has potentially committed a Computer Misuse Act offence and has tainted any evidence they find.

**Do not screenshot the complainant's WhatsApp without permission.** Even if the complainant has shown you the screen, you do not own the contents and you cannot copy them without consent. Sit down with the complainant, ask whether they want to share, let them send the screenshots themselves, and forward the originals to HR untouched. Chain of custody matters for both procedural fairness and any subsequent regulatory or criminal proceeding.

**Do not coach witnesses.** Don't tell other team members what you've heard. Don't ask them leading questions about the alleged perpetrator. Don't prepare anyone for an interview. This is the investigator's job, and managerial interference is itself a Conduct Rule risk.

**Do tell HR everything you know.** Including the bits that are inconvenient for the complainant or for you. Selective disclosure to HR is the manager mistake that turns a single incident into a much larger problem.

## Section 5: Managing the team during an investigation

Investigations take weeks. The team will speculate. Your job during that period is to hold the line, not to take sides.

**Presumption of innocence for the respondent.** The respondent has not yet been found to have done anything wrong. Treating them as guilty before the finding is itself a fairness breach and, where it materially affects them, may be actionable.

**Protection from victimisation for the complainant.** The complainant must not be moved out of meetings, reassigned away from prestigious work, frozen out socially, or subjected to subtle exclusion. See Section 6 for the performance-management dimension.

**Communication discipline.** No team-wide commentary on the matter. No "between us" with one party or the other. No reassurance to peers that you "know who you believe." The team will speculate; your job is to model: *I'm not going to discuss this, and I expect you to respect that too.* That is the only sustainable position.

**Workload and seating.** Don't transfer the complainant out of inconvenient meetings ("for their own comfort") and don't isolate the respondent ("until this is resolved"). Either action reads as outcome-determining. If a genuine separation is needed for safeguarding reasons, HR — not you — decides on it, and it is framed and recorded as a precautionary measure, not as a finding.

**Sociability with both parties.** This is awkward and there is no clean answer. The rule of thumb is: roughly equivalent treatment of both, no obvious favouring of one over the other, no exclusion from team events on the grounds of the investigation. If the discomfort is too much, HR can advise.

## Section 6: The single biggest manager-mistake the law punishes — performance management as retaliation

This is the section that matters most for managers, because it is the section where the largest tribunal awards land.

The pattern is consistent across decided cases:

- A complaint is filed against the manager, against the team, or against a peer the manager is aligned with.
- Subsequently, the complainant's next appraisal is harsher than the trajectory of the previous twelve months suggested.
- The complainant's bonus is lower than peers doing equivalent work.
- The complainant is redeployed to a less prestigious team, taken off a high-profile project, or excluded from meetings they previously attended.
- The manager, when challenged, gives a plausible-sounding business reason — performance concerns that "predated" the complaint, a restructure that was "already planned," a fit issue with the new project lead.

Every one of these reads, to a tribunal, as victimisation under s.27 Equality Act 2010. And from **6 April 2026**, where the underlying disclosure was a protected disclosure under the whistleblowing regime (s.43B Employment Rights Act 1996 as amended by s.23 ERA 2025), the same conduct is also actionable as **whistleblowing detriment with uncapped damages** — on top of any Equality Act claim.

Two things tribunals look for, and both work against the manager:

**Inferred causation.** Tribunals do not require explicit evidence that the manager mentioned the complaint when making the decision. They draw inferences from timing, from treatment relative to peers, and crucially from the absence of contemporaneous documentation of any alternative reason. If the underperformance was real, where was it raised before the complaint? If the bonus was justified, where is the calibration paperwork?

**Shifted burden of proof.** Under s.136 Equality Act 2010, once the complainant shows facts from which a tribunal could conclude that victimisation occurred, the burden shifts to the employer to show that the treatment was for a non-discriminatory reason. The manager is then in the position of having to prove a negative.

### The operational rule

**Any performance-management action against a complainant during or after an investigation must be reviewed by HR before it is taken.** Not after. Not optionally. Before.

The review covers three questions:

1. **Would this action have happened anyway?** Is there a paper trail predating the complaint that supports the action — prior reviews, prior conversations, prior calibration data? If yes, document the trail. If no, do not take the action.

2. **Is the treatment consistent with how comparable peers are treated?** If the complainant's bonus is being adjusted in a way no peer's is, that's an inferred-causation problem.

3. **Is there a less disadvantageous alternative?** If the same business need can be met without affecting the complainant negatively, take that route.

If you cannot answer all three positively with documentation, **do not take the action**. The cost of pausing for a quarter is much smaller than the cost of a victimisation claim with uncapped damages.

The same rule applies during the investigation period and for a reasonable period after it concludes — twelve months is a sensible internal benchmark. The protection does not lapse when the investigation does.

## Section 7: Third-party harassment of your team (October 2026)

From **1 October 2026** (s.21 Employment Rights Act 2025), the firm becomes directly liable for harassment of its employees by **third parties** — clients, contractors, suppliers, customers, visitors, brokers, anyone the team encounters through their work — across all protected characteristics, not just sex. This reinstates the third-party regime that was repealed in 2013 and expands it materially.

For managers this means:

**The harassment of your team member by an external party is the firm's liability, not just theirs.** If your underwriter is being subjected to inappropriate comments by a broker on calls and the firm does not act, the firm is in breach. Not the broker; the firm.

**The escalation route is firm-side, not employee-side.** The right move is rarely "have a quiet word with the junior to manage the client" — that often itself amounts to victimisation (asking the complainant to put up with the conduct in order to preserve a relationship). The right moves are firm-side actions: pause the relationship pending review, issue a formal warning to the third party, decline renewal, terminate the engagement, document the pattern.

**Risk assessment is part of your job.** EHRC Step 3 specifically calls out customer-facing risk as a category to assess. If your team has a known problem client or a known problem broker, you have a duty to flag it and to plan around it — not to leave it to the next junior to absorb.

### Worked example — broker calls

An underwriter on your team tells you that a broker — call him James at Acme Brokers — has been making sexual comments on calls. She thinks it's been going on for about two months. She didn't want to raise it because Acme is in the top 10 of the firm's broker book and she didn't want to make a fuss.

You have a duty to act. From 1 October 2026, the firm's exposure is direct. From 1 September 2026, your handling of it is in scope of your own COCON 1.1.7FR obligation.

The escalation route is:

1. **Acknowledge and thank her.** She has done the hard part by telling you.
2. **Notes, contemporaneous, to yourself.** Date, what she said, what she wants from you.
3. **Reassure her on retaliation** — both about the broker (she will not be re-allocated as a punishment for raising this) and inside the firm (her performance and progression are unaffected).
4. **Same-day escalation to HR + your manager.** The firm-side action is not yours alone to decide.
5. **Preserve the evidence** — Teams call recordings, calendar entries, any chat correspondence with the broker. Do not delete anything. Forensic IT pulls the recordings, not you.
6. **Take the underwriter off direct contact with James** — but only with her agreement, and framed explicitly as a precaution, not as a finding against her or as a workload demotion.
7. **Firm-side action to the broker** — the relationship-management lead, the head of underwriting, and HR / Compliance jointly decide whether the response is a formal warning to the broker firm, a named-broker exclusion, suspension of the relationship pending an internal review at Acme, or termination. Document the decision.

The wrong move is: *try to handle James yourself, see if it tails off, swap the underwriter onto a different broker for "balance," and don't tell HR.* That is the sequence that lands in a tribunal exhibit bundle.

## Section 8: The FCA / SMCR layer for managers and SMCR-certified staff

This section applies to every manager and every certified person, because most managers in the Group are at least one of the two.

**You are personally accountable for culture under COCON 1.1.7FR from 1 September 2026.** Bullying, harassment and violence against colleagues — across all SMCR-regulated firms — are in scope of the Conduct Rules. The seriousness threshold is the Equality Act test, verbatim. A substantiated finding against you is a Conduct Rule breach in your own name, separate from any underlying allegation against anyone else.

**Your firm has a 7-business-day reporting clock under SUP 10C.14 for SMF Conduct Rule breaches.** If you are an SMF and you are aware of a Conduct Rule breach — yours or another SMF's — the clock starts when the firm becomes aware. You don't get to defer the clock by deferring the conversation with HR.

**REP008 / Form H annually for certified persons.** Reportable disciplinary actions (formal warning, suspension, dismissal, remuneration reduction or recovery) are aggregated and reported by 31 October each year for the period to 31 August. Internal resolution does not remove the reporting obligation; it triggers it.

**SUP 15.3 / Principle 11 catch-all.** Anything serious enough that the FCA "would reasonably expect notice." Credible allegations against a senior executive, media exposure, a pattern of complaints — any of these can trigger a SUP 15.3 notification independent of the other clocks.

**SYSC 22 regulatory references — the pass-the-harasser loophole closes 1 September 2026.** Substantiated NFM findings travel with the individual when they move firm. This applies to you as well as to anyone you might be investigating. A substantiated finding against you is disclosed in your regulatory reference when you move to another regulated firm. The Crispin Odey precedent — £1.8 million fine and an industry ban under Individual Conduct Rule 1 for conduct around the handling of sexual-harassment allegations — is the public proof that the FCA will use these tools at the most senior level.

You don't need to memorise the rule numbers. You do need to know that the regulator is now downstream of any HR finding involving you or your team, and that managerial mishandling — your own — is in regulatory scope.

## Section 9: When the manager is the complainant or the respondent

A manager will sometimes be the person raising a concern, or the person a concern is raised about. The rules in that case are short and they are non-negotiable.

**Stand down from any investigatory role.** You cannot investigate a matter you are part of. Defer immediately to HR or to Speak Up.

**Don't access the team's data.** Emails, chats, calendar invitations, performance records relating to the other party or to potential witnesses are off-limits to you until the matter is resolved. Accessing them, even out of professional habit, taints the evidential chain and is itself a Conduct Rule risk.

**Don't try to influence witnesses.** Don't approach team members to discuss the matter, even casually. Don't ask "what did you see" questions. Don't reassure people about what they should remember. The investigator will speak to them.

**The firm will appoint an investigator who is not in your reporting line.** That may be an external investigator. You will be told who it is. You should expect the same procedural-fairness protections as any other respondent or complainant — the substance of the allegation in writing, the chance to respond, access to a colleague or representative, a clear timeline.

**Your day job continues, with adjustments.** Where the matter is serious, HR may agree precautionary adjustments to your duties — typically removing direct line-management of the other party, or removing access to specific systems. These are precautionary measures, not findings. Their purpose is to protect the integrity of the investigation, not to pre-judge it.

## Section 10: What to take from this module

- Receiving a disclosure is a named skill. Listen, thank, take contemporaneous notes, tell them what happens next, don't promise outcomes, don't promise absolute confidentiality, don't investigate yourself, don't tell peers, don't pre-warn the alleged perpetrator.
- The "they don't want a formal complaint" dilemma resolves through HR consultation, not through silence and not through unilateral escalation. The complainant's wishes shape how you act; they don't dispose of your duty.
- The duty to act does not depend on a formal complaint. s.40A Equality Act 2010, EHRC Step 3, and (from 1 September 2026) COCON 1.1.7FR all attach to awareness, not paperwork.
- Evidence preservation: contemporaneous notes, don't delete anything, don't access the alleged perpetrator's accounts, don't screenshot the complainant's phone, don't coach witnesses.
- Performance management as retaliation is the single largest tribunal-award risk. Any performance action against a complainant during or after an investigation must be reviewed by HR first — would it have happened anyway, is it consistent with peers, is there a less disadvantageous alternative.
- Third-party harassment of your team becomes direct firm liability from 1 October 2026 (s.21 ERA 2025). Escalation is firm-side, not "have a quiet word with the junior."
- The FCA reporting clocks (SUP 10C.14, REP008, SUP 15.3) and SYSC 22 regulatory references run in parallel to HR. From 1 September 2026, your own handling of NFM is in COCON 1.1.7FR scope.
- If you are the complainant or the respondent: stand down, don't access team data, don't approach witnesses, expect external investigation.

## Reflection prompts

Three open-text prompts. **Logged but not scored. Your line manager will never see your answers** — only Group HR, and only in aggregate themes.

1. Think about the last time a team member came to you with something that was uncomfortable — not necessarily harassment, but something that needed handling. Looking back through the framework in Section 2, what would you have done differently?

2. The "they don't want a formal complaint" trap in Section 2 has no clean answer. If you found yourself in that situation tomorrow, who specifically — by name or by role — would be your first call for advice, and is that person actually available to you?

3. Look honestly at the last performance decision you made about someone on your team — appraisal score, bonus recommendation, project allocation. If that person were to raise a harassment concern next week and the decision had to be defended in a tribunal context (Section 6), is the paper trail there? If not, what would you change about how you document these decisions going forward?
