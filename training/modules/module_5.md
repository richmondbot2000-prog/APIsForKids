# Module 5 — Reporting and what happens next

**Duration:** ~10 minutes reading + 8-question test

---

## Section 1: Two routes, neither of them lesser

The single biggest reason people don't report harassment is not fear of the perpetrator. It is not knowing what happens to *them* once they speak. This module exists to answer that.

There are two routes. **Informal** and **formal.** They are not a ladder — neither one is the senior version of the other. They are different tools for different situations, and most matters are resolved through the informal route without ever needing the formal one.

**Informal route.** A conversation. You raise the matter with your line manager, your skip-level manager, an HR business partner, or the confidential Speak Up channel. Nothing is written down by you. The person you speak to logs the disclosure (they have to — see Module M, the manager track), but the framing is open: you may be testing the air, you may be seeking advice, you may want the conduct to stop without a formal process. The person hearing the disclosure should help you think through the options, not push you to a destination. Informal routes routinely resolve matters — a quiet word with the person concerned, a change of seating, a pause on a working relationship, a manager-mediated conversation. None of that is a lesser outcome.

**Formal route.** A written grievance. The firm appoints an investigator (HR or external), interviews are held, evidence is gathered, a finding is made, and an outcome is communicated to both parties. The formal route is appropriate where the conduct is serious, where informal steps have not worked, where the alleged perpetrator is senior and the power gradient makes informal action impractical, or where you want a documented finding for any reason.

You may move from informal to formal at any point. You do not need to commit at the start.

## Section 2: Confidentiality — what is and isn't protected

The firm cannot promise absolute confidentiality. Anyone who says otherwise is wrong, and the assurance won't survive contact with the first regulator.

What the firm can promise:

- Information about your disclosure is shared only on a **need-to-know basis** — typically HR, the investigator, and the relevant senior manager. It is not shared with your line, with peers, or with the team.
- If a disclosure must be made to anyone beyond that group (the FCA, the police, an external regulator, a respondent's new employer through a regulatory reference), you will be told **before** the disclosure is made, not after.
- Your reflection-prompt answers in this training are visible to Group HR only, never to your line manager. Module M makes the same commitment to managers about their reflections.

What the firm cannot promise:

- That the **respondent** (the person you've raised a concern about) will never know who raised it. Procedural fairness requires that a respondent be told the substance of the allegation so they can respond, and in most cases the substance is enough to identify the complainant. Anonymous reporting is available through the Speak Up channel, but anonymous complaints are harder to investigate and harder to act on. You will not be pushed in either direction; the choice is yours.
- That nothing will be reported to the FCA. Three regulatory reporting clocks (Section 5) are independent of your wishes. You should know about them in advance — see below.
- That nothing will be reported to the police. Where conduct may amount to a criminal offence (sexual assault, threats, stalking), the firm will support you in reporting it but will not generally report on your behalf without your agreement except where there is an immediate risk to others.

## Section 3: No retaliation — and the lever changed in April 2026

Retaliation against someone who raises a harassment complaint, or who supports someone else's complaint, is unlawful under **section 27 of the Equality Act 2010** (victimisation). That has been the position since 2010 and it is well-established: a complainant who is subsequently passed over for promotion, given a harsher appraisal, redeployed to a less prestigious team or excluded from meetings, and who can show that the change followed the complaint, can bring a victimisation claim alongside the underlying harassment claim.

The bigger lever sits on top of that, and it is new.

Since **6 April 2026**, any qualifying disclosure relating to sexual harassment is a **protected disclosure** under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (as inserted into s.43B Employment Rights Act 1996 by s.23 Employment Rights Act 2025). The consequence: detriment or dismissal in response to such a disclosure is a whistleblowing claim with **uncapped damages** — on top of any Equality Act claim. There is no statutory cap on the compensation a tribunal can award.

One caveat to understand. The whistleblowing regime carries a **public-interest test**: the discloser must reasonably believe the disclosure is in the public interest. A complaint about a pattern of conduct, a repeat offender, multiple victims, or a systemic failure clearly qualifies. A complaint about a one-off, personal incident affecting only the discloser is in greyer territory and may not automatically qualify as a protected disclosure — though it remains a protected act under s.27 Equality Act 2010. In practice, most harassment in a workplace has at least a pattern dimension, and tribunals have read the public-interest test broadly since the 2017 *Chesterton* decision.

For everyone reading this: the practical takeaway is that retaliating against a complainant is now one of the most expensive single mistakes a firm or a manager can make in UK employment law.

## Section 4: What an investigation actually looks like

The mystery is the friction. Once you know the shape, the friction drops.

**Step 1 — Receipt.** Your disclosure is logged by whoever received it. You are told within two working days who will own the matter from here.

**Step 2 — Investigator appointed.** The firm names an investigator — usually an HR business partner, sometimes external for senior or complex cases. The investigator is not in your reporting line and not in the respondent's. You are told who it is.

**Step 3 — Scope agreed.** The investigator meets you and confirms what's being investigated, what witnesses you suggest, what evidence you can offer, and what outcome you are seeking. You can have a colleague or trade-union representative with you. Notes are taken and shared with you for accuracy.

**Step 4 — Interviews.** The investigator interviews the respondent and any witnesses. The respondent is given the substance of the allegation so they can respond, but witness identities are protected where possible.

**Step 5 — Finding.** The investigator reaches a finding — usually one of: substantiated, partially substantiated, not substantiated on the evidence, or insufficient evidence. The finding is communicated to both parties.

**Step 6 — Outcome.** Where the finding is upheld in whole or part, the outcome is decided by HR / a hearing manager — ranging from informal conversation to formal warning to dismissal. Where the respondent is SMCR-regulated, the outcome interacts with the FCA reporting clocks in Section 5.

**Typical timeline.** Two to six weeks from receipt to finding for a straightforward matter. Longer for complex cases or where the respondent is senior. You are kept informed in writing at each stage. If the investigation is going to take materially longer, you are told why.

You may withdraw the complaint at any point. Withdrawal does not always stop the investigation — see Section 7 of Module M for the manager-side dilemma — but your wishes are weighted heavily.

## Section 5: The FCA layer — three reporting clocks the firm has to keep

If you work for an FCA-regulated firm (and almost everyone reading this does), the firm has reporting obligations to the FCA that run in parallel to its obligations to you. You should know about them because they mean the FCA may hear about a finding even if it is resolved internally — and because it explains why HR cannot promise the matter "stays inside the firm."

Three regimes interlock:

- **SUP 10C.14 — seven-business-day clock.** Where the firm becomes aware that an approved Senior Manager (SMF) has breached a Conduct Rule — which now includes serious harassment under COCON 1.1.7FR from 1 September 2026 — the firm must notify the FCA via Form D within **seven business days**.

- **REP008 / Form H — annual return.** Conduct Rule breaches by certified persons (everyone in SMCR scope other than SMFs) are reported annually via REP008. Reporting period 1 September – 31 August; deadline **31 October**. Reportable disciplinary actions are formal written warnings, suspension, dismissal, and remuneration reduction or recovery.

- **SUP 15.3 — Principle 11 catch-all.** Anything serious enough that the FCA "would reasonably expect notice" — for example, a credible allegation against a senior executive, a media disclosure, a pattern of complaints — can trigger a SUP 15.3 notification independent of the other two clocks.

Layered over those: **SYSC 22 regulatory references.** From **1 September 2026**, substantiated non-financial-misconduct findings — including harassment, bullying and violence — must be disclosed in regulatory references when an individual moves regulated firm. This is what closes the "pass the harasser" loophole. A respondent who is found to have harassed a colleague at Richmond Group cannot move to another regulated firm and have that finding stay behind.

You do not need to memorise the rule numbers. You need to know that the regulator may hear about a finding, that substantiated findings travel with the respondent, and that the firm is not in a position to make the matter disappear.

## Section 6: Where to report — seven routes, not one

The complainant chooses. There is no "right" first stop.

- **Your line manager.** The default for most matters. They are trained in disclosure-receiving (see Module M) and have an immediate duty to act.

- **A skip-level manager.** Use this where your direct line manager is the respondent, is in a relationship with the respondent, or where the matter touches your direct team in a way that makes your line manager the wrong person to approach first.

- **HR — the named contact.** A named HR business partner is published on the Directory page under your business area. They can take the disclosure directly, advise you informally, or route it appropriately.

- **The Speak Up channel.** The firm's confidential reporting route, including an anonymous option. Available 24/7. Run through an independent provider — disclosures are not screened by line management before reaching the Speak Up triage team.

- **The EHRC.** The Equality and Human Rights Commission is the external regulator for the Equality Act. They can investigate the firm directly and act without an individual complainant. You can contact them whether or not you have raised the matter internally.

- **ACAS helpline.** **0300 123 1100.** Free, confidential employment-rights advice. ACAS sits between informal grievance and formal tribunal and is the body the law will direct you to before a tribunal claim through early conciliation.

- **The police.** Where the conduct may be a criminal offence — sexual assault, threats, stalking, malicious communications — 999 in emergency, 101 otherwise. The firm will support a criminal report; it does not replace it with an internal process.

Seven routes. You pick.

## Section 7: "Have I done the right thing?"

Yes.

If something sat oddly with you and you raised it, you have used the system the way it is meant to be used. The investigation process is designed to weigh evidence; it is not designed to punish complainants whose evidence does not, on examination, support the conclusion they reached at the time. A complaint that is not upheld, or that is found to have a different shape than the complainant believed, is not a failed complaint. It is the system doing what it is supposed to do.

Two specific points.

**Good-faith complaints that turn out to be unfounded are protected.** Section 27(3) of the Equality Act 2010 only removes protection from allegations made in **bad faith** — knowingly false, or made for an improper purpose. A complaint that you genuinely believed when you raised it, but that the evidence ultimately did not support, is fully protected. You cannot be victimised for it.

**The bar for "bad faith" is high.** It requires evidence that you knew the allegation was untrue or that you made it for a collateral reason (to damage someone professionally, to deflect from your own conduct). Being mistaken, being upset, misremembering details, or being unable to prove the matter on the balance of probabilities does not begin to approach bad faith.

If you are uncertain whether to raise something, the right call is almost always to raise it. The cost of raising a concern that turns out to be ambiguous is small; the cost of staying silent on something that turns out to be serious is large — for the complainant, for the next victim, and for the firm.

## Section 8: What to take from this module

- Two routes: informal and formal. Informal is not lesser. You can move between them.
- Confidentiality is need-to-know, not absolute. The firm tells you before any disclosure outside that circle is made.
- Retaliation is unlawful — s.27 Equality Act 2010, and (since 6 April 2026) sexual-harassment disclosures are protected whistleblowing disclosures with uncapped damages for detriment or dismissal.
- An investigation typically runs two to six weeks. You are kept informed in writing at each stage.
- Three FCA reporting clocks run in parallel — SUP 10C.14 (7-day Form D for SMFs), REP008/Form H (annual for certified persons), SUP 15.3 (Principle 11 catch-all). SYSC 22 regulatory references close the pass-the-harasser loophole from 1 September 2026.
- Six reporting routes: line manager, skip-level, HR, Speak Up, EHRC, ACAS (0300 123 1100), police. You choose.
- Good-faith complaints are protected even where they are not upheld. The bar for "bad faith" requires evidence of knowing falsity or improper purpose.

## Reflection prompts

Two open-text prompts. **Logged but not scored. Your line manager will never see your answers** — only Group HR, and only in aggregate themes.

1. If something happened at work tomorrow that you thought might cross the line, which of the seven routes in Section 6 would you actually use first, and why? If the honest answer is "none of them," what would have to change for that to be different?

2. Think about the last time you considered raising a concern at work — about harassment or anything else — and decided not to. What was the deciding factor? You don't have to describe the incident, just the factor.
