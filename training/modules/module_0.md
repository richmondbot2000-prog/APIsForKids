# Module 0 — Professional conduct in digital channels

**Duration:** ~5 minutes reading + 8-question test

---

## Section 1: The rules don't change because you're typing

Almost everything you do at work now leaves a written record. Teams, Slack, WhatsApp, email, calendar invites, the reactions you tap on someone else's message — all of it is logged somewhere, often by default, and most of it can be retrieved years later.

The Equality Act 2010 makes no distinction between conduct delivered out loud and conduct delivered through a keyboard. A comment that would be harassment in the broker dialler row is harassment when it lands in the broker WhatsApp group. The medium does not soften the test. The s.26 three-limb test — **unwanted conduct, related to a protected characteristic, with the purpose or effect of violating dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment** — applies to a GIF as readily as it applies to a sentence.

If you took Module 1, the framework is the same. This module covers how that framework plays out in the channels you actually use all day.

## Section 2: "It's the banter group, it's not work"

The most common defence after a screenshotted group chat surfaces is some version of "that wasn't a work channel." It rarely holds.

Three things are routinely misunderstood about work-adjacent chats.

**The channel doesn't have to be official.** Employment tribunals and the FCA both look at substance, not branding. A WhatsApp group called "the lads" with twelve colleagues in it, set up to coordinate Friday drinks, is treated as a work channel the moment work topics, work people, or work-day timing appear in it. The 2024 Bull Express tribunal and successive FCA decisions have made this point repeatedly: if the participants are colleagues and the content touches on the workplace or its people, it is in scope.

**Silent members are not bystanders.** If you are in the group when something is posted, "I wasn't the one who sent it" and "I didn't react" are not safe positions. The s.26 effect test asks whether a reasonable person would consider the conduct had the effect of creating a hostile environment. A junior in a group of fifteen who watches an objectifying image of a female colleague get nine laughing-emoji reactions does not need anyone to address her by name for the environment to be hostile. Leaving the group, flagging it, or speaking up are the routes that protect both her and you.

**Screenshots travel.** WhatsApp is end-to-end encrypted between devices. It is not encrypted between a screenshot and the rest of the world. Since 6 April 2026, a colleague who passes a screenshot of a sexist group chat to HR or the FCA is making a **protected disclosure under the Employment Rights Act 1996** (as amended by s.23 ERA 2025). Retaliation against that person — exclusion, performance write-ups, withholding work, freezing them out — carries **uncapped damages**, on top of any Equality Act claim.

**Scenario.** A broker WhatsApp group called "Dialler Top 5" exists between six senior brokers, mostly used to chase each other on weekly numbers. After a Friday pub trip, someone posts a photo taken of a junior female broker's screen — she has been visibly upset on the floor that week — with a caption about "the new starter who can't take a joke." Three of the five react with laughing emojis. The other two say nothing. Two weeks later HR receives the screenshot. The two silent members are within scope of the investigation, not because they wrote anything, but because their continued membership of the chat after that post is relevant to whether the firm took all reasonable steps and to whether each individual met the FCA Conduct Rules.

## Section 3: Reactions, GIFs and images

Reactions and GIFs feel disposable because they take a tenth of a second to send. They aren't disposable. They are evidence in exactly the same format as text, and they often carry the content the text was too cautious to deliver explicitly.

A laughing-face reaction to a colleague's comment about another colleague's appearance, accent, religious dress, disability or pregnancy is participation in the conduct. The reaction is logged, attributed to your account, and timestamped. In an investigation it is read alongside the message it was attached to, not in isolation.

GIFs deserve their own warning. The GIF libraries built into Teams, Slack and WhatsApp are populated largely from sources that are not curated against the Equality Act. They contain ethnic stereotypes, objectified images of women, mocking representations of disability, transphobic memes, and material that would never survive a five-minute review by anyone in HR. Searching "secretary" or "boss" or any of a dozen body-related terms returns content that meets the s.26 test on its face. The platform's autocomplete is not a defence; you chose to send it.

**Scenario.** A trader posts in a Teams channel of about thirty people that she will be on maternity leave from October. A colleague responds with a GIF of a heavily pregnant woman waddling and dropping a coffee. He intends it warmly. Half the channel laughs, three react with the heart emoji, one DMs her to ask if she is OK. The intent doesn't matter. The s.26(4) test asks whether a reasonable person would consider the conduct had the effect of being humiliating or degrading on the basis of pregnancy. The answer is yes, and the conduct was performed in a written work channel with thirty witnesses. The fact she "laughed it off" in the moment is not the end of the analysis — see Module 1, §3.

## Section 4: DMs, late-night messages and the end of a workplace relationship

One-to-one channels carry the same rules, with one extra risk: there are no witnesses to keep behaviour in check, and the pace can escalate without anyone visible noticing.

Three patterns recur in tribunal and FCA cases.

**Persistent contact after a clear decline.** Asking a colleague out once is not harassment. Asking again after a polite refusal is awkward. Continuing after a firm refusal meets the s.26 test as persistent unwanted conduct of a sexual or romantic nature. The medium — Teams DM, WhatsApp, LinkedIn — is immaterial.

**Out-of-hours messaging with a power gradient.** Senior people DMing junior people at 11pm, particularly after deal-closing drinks or a long client lunch, sits in a tribunal exhibit bundle uncomfortably even when the words themselves are innocuous. If you are senior to the recipient, the message is being read in the context of that power relationship, not in isolation. "Are you up?" texts from a managing director to a graduate are not interpreted by a tribunal the way they are by the sender.

**The end of a workplace relationship.** Consensual relationships at work end. When they do, continued unsolicited contact from one party to the other through any work channel — Teams, work email, "accidentally" added to a project group — is exactly the territory s.26 was written for. If you would not be comfortable with the messages being read aloud at a disciplinary hearing or printed in a regulatory reference, do not send them.

**Scenario.** A senior associate and an analyst on the same M&A team end a six-month relationship. The associate continues to send personal messages to the analyst through Teams over the next month, including after the analyst asks them to stop. The analyst raises it with their line manager. Because the messages are work-channel, work-hours-adjacent, and continue after a clear refusal, this is potential sexual harassment under s.26(2) — and, separately, a potential FCA Conduct Rule breach for the associate under COCON 1.1.7FR once that rule lands on 1 September 2026.

## Section 5: Calls, screenshares and the small leaks

The remaining category is the slip — the moment the mic was on, the moment a DM was open during a screenshare, the moment a Slack notification preview appeared mid-presentation.

**Cameras off, mic on.** Muting at the end of a call and unmuting for the next one is the daily failure mode. If you have just said something on a private call that you would not say on the main one, assume you said it on the main one until you have checked. The "hot mic" is now common enough in tribunal evidence bundles that it has its own column.

**Screenshares.** When you screenshare, close everything other than the document or app you are sharing. Teams notifications, Slack previews, an inbox subject line, a DM with a colleague's name in it — all of these surface in front of clients, regulators and other colleagues. A flash of "[Colleague] is a nightmare" in a chat preview during a client deck is not undone by closing the window.

**Screenshots of unsent material.** Drafting a sarcastic message you do not intend to send is a normal human pressure-release. Screenshotting it and sending the screenshot is what gets people sacked. The draft was hypothetical; the screenshot is conduct.

## Section 6: The FCA dimension in 30 seconds

If you work for an FCA-regulated firm — and almost everyone reading this does — anything in a written channel can end up in a regulatory reference.

From **1 September 2026**, the new COCON 1.1.7FR rule extends the SMCR Conduct Rules to bullying, harassment and violence against colleagues across all regulated firms. The seriousness threshold is the Equality Act test, verbatim. A substantiated finding sits on your record, is disclosable in references under SYSC 22 when you move firm, and is directly relevant to the annual fit-and-proper assessment under FIT 2.1. The FCA has made clear that "private" conduct can matter where it shows a willingness to disregard ethical obligations or abuse a position of trust — and a work-adjacent WhatsApp group is not private in the sense the regulator means.

You do not need to remember the rule numbers. You need to remember that the screenshot reaches the regulator, and the regulator has the power to make you unemployable in financial services.

## Section 7: What to take from this module

- Digital channels are governed by exactly the same harassment law as anything said out loud — the s.26 test does not care about the medium.
- Work-adjacent WhatsApp / Slack / Teams chats are routinely admitted as evidence; "it wasn't a work channel" almost never holds.
- Reactions and GIFs are conduct, not punctuation. A laughing emoji on the wrong message is participation.
- Silent members of a group chat are within scope; "I didn't say anything" is not a defence and may be its own problem.
- One-to-one channels carry the same rules, plus the added risk of no witnesses. Out-of-hours DMs across a power gradient sit badly in any investigation.
- Since 6 April 2026, passing a screenshot of a sexist or discriminatory group chat to HR is a protected whistleblowing disclosure with **uncapped** damages for retaliation.
- From 1 September 2026, the FCA Conduct Rules cover bullying and harassment in writing as readily as in person. A substantiated finding follows you between firms.

## Reflection prompts

Two open-text prompts. **Logged but not scored. Your line manager will never see your answers** — only Group HR, and only in aggregate themes.

1. Think of the last work-adjacent group chat you joined (WhatsApp, Signal, a Teams side-channel, anything). If a transcript of the last month of that chat landed on the desk of the Group CEO tomorrow, is there anything in it you would want to explain in advance? You don't have to write what it is — just whether the answer is yes or no, and what you might do about it.

2. Have you ever stayed silent in a group chat when something was posted that you found uncomfortable? What stopped you from saying something, and what would have made it easier?
