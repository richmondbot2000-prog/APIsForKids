# Harassment-prevention training: what the evidence actually shows

Research summary for TogetherBook / Richmond Group | 2026-05-20

---

## 0. Headline correction on the "IFS findings"

**The CEO's citation appears to be a misattribution.** A direct search of the Institute for Fiscal Studies catalogue (ifs.org.uk) returns no publication on the effectiveness of workplace harassment training. The IFS has published one adjacent piece — *"The economic consequences of gender-based violence"* (2026) — which addresses the **costs** of GBV to victims and the economy, not training efficacy. A search engine summary that surfaced a "2020 IFS report... 18% increase in under-reporting" does not correspond to any real IFS output and should be treated as a hallucinated citation; do not put that number into the research summary.

What the CEO is almost certainly remembering is one of three real bodies of work that *are* widely quoted in UK HR/D&I circles:

1. **Dobbin & Kalev (Harvard / Tel Aviv), Harvard Business Review 2016** — "Why Diversity Programs Fail." *Mandatory* training data over 30 years across 800+ US firms.
2. **EEOC Select Task Force on the Study of Harassment in the Workplace (Feldblum & Lipnic, 2016)** — the most comprehensive evidence review by a regulator.
3. **Behavioural Insights Team (UK Cabinet Office spin-out)** — review of UBT/diversity training evidence; explicitly recommends *against* mandatory tick-box training.

These are the sources to anchor the brief on. The lines below distinguish carefully between them.

---

## 1. Dobbin & Kalev — "Why Diversity Programs Fail" (HBR, July–August 2016)

Longitudinal data on **829 mid-sized and large US firms, 1971–2002**, supplemented by interviews. The single most-cited body of evidence on training failure.

Verbatim:

> "Five years after instituting required training for managers, companies saw no improvement in the proportion of white women, Black men, and Hispanics in management, and the share of Black women actually **decreased by 9 %**, on average, while the ranks of Asian American men and women **shrank by 4 % to 5 %**."

> "People often rebel against rules to assert their autonomy. Try to coerce me to do X, Y, or Z, and I'll do the opposite just to prove that I'm my own person."

By contrast, **voluntary** training produced 9–13 % increases in representation of several minority groups over the same five years. Their seminal 2006 paper *Best Practices or Best Guesses?* (Kalev, Dobbin & Kelly, *American Sociological Review* 71:589–617) established that **structures that create accountability** — diversity task-forces, mentoring programmes, dedicated diversity managers — drive change. **Training and grievance systems alone, especially when mandatory, drive backlash.**

Source: [hbr.org/2016/07/why-diversity-programs-fail](https://hbr.org/2016/07/why-diversity-programs-fail); [scholar.harvard.edu/dobbin](https://dobbin.scholars.harvard.edu/publications/why-diversity-programs-fail); ASR 2006: [journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/000312240607100404](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/000312240607100404)

## 2. EEOC Select Task Force on Harassment in the Workplace (Feldblum & Lipnic, 2016)

Eighteen-month evidence review by a 16-member panel of academics, practitioners and labour reps; the most authoritative single regulator document on training design.

Verbatim:

> "Much of the training done over the last 30 years has not worked as a prevention tool — it's been too focused on simply avoiding legal liability."

> "Workplace culture has the greatest impact on allowing harassment to flourish, or conversely, in preventing harassment."

> "The importance of leadership cannot be overstated — effective harassment prevention efforts, and workplace culture in which harassment is not tolerated, must start with and involve the highest level of management."

> "Training is most effective when **tailored to the specific workforce and workplace**, and to different cohorts of employees."

> Bystander intervention training can "empower co-workers and give them the tools to intervene when they witness harassing behavior, and may show promise for harassment prevention."

> "Employers should periodically 'test' their reporting system to determine how well the system is working."

Core recommendations: leader-led, repeated, context-specific, with bystander and civility training, **and** with effectiveness measured by reporting rates and complainant experience, not completion rates.

Source: [eeoc.gov/select-task-force-study-harassment-workplace-report-co-chairs-chai-r-feldblum-victoria-lipnic](https://www.eeoc.gov/select-task-force-study-harassment-workplace-report-co-chairs-chai-r-feldblum-victoria-lipnic); full PDF: [eeoc.gov/sites/default/files/migrated_files/eeoc/task_force/harassment/report.pdf](https://www.eeoc.gov/sites/default/files/migrated_files/eeoc/task_force/harassment/report.pdf)

## 3. Bezrukova et al. — 40-year meta-analysis (Psychological Bulletin, 2016)

260 independent samples. The strongest *quantitative* synthesis. Overall effect Hedges g = 0.38, but heavily concentrated in *reactions* and *cognitive learning* — i.e. people understand the rules in the room. **Behavioural and attitudinal effects are markedly smaller and decay quickly.** The three moderators that turn effects positive and lasting:

- Training **complemented by other diversity initiatives** (consistent with Dobbin & Kalev: structure beats content).
- **Both awareness AND skills** targeted, not just one.
- **Sustained over time**, not a one-off.

Source: [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27618543](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27618543/)

## 4. Behavioural Insights Team — "Unconscious bias and diversity training: the evidence"

BIT (the UK Cabinet Office behavioural-economics unit) is the closest UK analogue the CEO may be thinking of when he says "IFS." Their public position:

> "Studies are yet to rigorously show that UBT and diversity training change biased behaviours in the workplace in any lasting way."

> "Avoid mandatory, 'one-off,' tick-the-box training sessions which risk making the problem worse."

They explicitly identify the **moral-licensing** mechanism: completion of "good" training increases licence to make biased decisions afterwards. BIT recommends investing instead in structural fixes — anonymised CVs, structured interviews, transparent promotion criteria, diverse shortlists.

Source: [bi.team/blogs/unconscious-bias-and-diversity-training-the-evidence](https://www.bi.team/blogs/unconscious-bias-and-diversity-training-the-evidence/)

## 5. Active-bystander research and the 5Ds framework

**Origin.** The 3Ds (Direct / Distract / Delegate) were pioneered by **Green Dot** (Dorothy Edwards, University of Kentucky, 2006–). **Hollaback! / Right To Be** partnered with Green Dot in 2012 and added **Delay (2015)** and **Document (2017)** to produce the 5Ds now used internationally.

**Evidence base.**

- The Green Dot multi-college cluster RCT (Coker et al., American Journal of Preventive Medicine) found **17–21 % lower interpersonal-violence victimisation** in trained vs control high schools and colleges.
- A 2020 BMJ Open UK community evaluation of bystander training found large-effect-size improvements in bystander self-efficacy, behavioural intent and rejection of myth acceptance at follow-up. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7218832](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7218832/)
- A 2024 scoping review in Annals of Work Exposures and Health concludes bystander programmes are "promising" but warns the workplace (as opposed to campus) evidence base is still thin and most studies lack RCT design. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12161231](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12161231/)

The 5Ds also reframe the *learner*: instead of "do not be a harasser" (which provokes reactance in 95 % of staff who never were) it asks "what would you do if you saw it?" — a question virtually everyone answers willingly.

Source: [righttobe.org/guides/bystander-intervention-training](https://righttobe.org/guides/bystander-intervention-training/)

## 6. Behavioural-science findings relevant to compliance training

| Finding | Mechanism | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **Spacing effect** | Material studied across multiple spaced sessions is retained 2–3× longer than equivalent total time massed in one session. Replicated across 10,500-person workplace dataset. | Cepeda et al., *Psych Bulletin* 2006; Latimier et al. 2021 — [link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13428-018-1184-7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13428-018-1184-7) |
| **Testing effect** | Active retrieval — even low-stakes — outperforms re-reading for long-term retention by ~50 %. | Roediger & Karpicke, *Psych Science* 2006 |
| **Plausible scenarios beat abstract rules** | Concrete cases activate situational reasoning; abstract policy statements rarely transfer. Particularly strong for compliance content. | Clark, *Scenario-Based e-Learning* 2013 |
| **Manager-specific tracks raise manager confidence** | Managers face *role-specific* dilemmas (handling a disclosure, intervening across hierarchy) absent from generic content; targeted content closes self-efficacy gap. | EEOC Task Force 2016 §V.C.3 |
| **Anonymous reflection prompts improve recall** | Self-generation of content (writing your own answer) outperforms passive reception (Slamecka & Graf "generation effect"); anonymity removes social-desirability filter that distorts forced-choice items. | Slamecka & Graf 1978; replicated in compliance contexts by Kraiger 2014 |

## 7. Negative findings to be honest about

**Moral licensing.** Completing "the harassment module" produces a *credentialing* effect: subjects rate themselves as less prejudiced, then behave *more* permissively in subsequent decisions. (Monin & Miller, *JPSP* 2001; replicated in DEI context by Castilla & Benard 2010.)

**Mandatory training backlash — gender-differential.** Tinkler, Gremillion & Arthurs (*Social Forces* 2015) and Bingham & Scherer (*Sex Roles* 2001) and Dobbin & Kalev 2019 (PNAS, *"The promise and peril of sexual harassment programs"*, [pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1818477116](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1818477116)) document:

- Men exposed to traditional training were **more** likely to blame victims and **less** likely to label coercion of a subordinate as harassment.
- Men reported reduced willingness to mentor or be alone with junior women post-training — the so-called "Pence effect."
- Women in firms with heavy formal training reported *more* harassment, not less — likely a reporting effect, but it means the headline metric ("incident count") *rises* in companies that train well, which leadership must be prepared for.

**Decay.** Bezrukova's meta-analysis: attitudinal effects of training fade within weeks. This is the single strongest argument against the annual-marathon format.

## 8. What an evidence-based programme looks like — concrete components

| Component | Why | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Spaced doses** — 4 × 15-min modules quarterly, not one 60-min annual block | Spacing + decay evidence | Cepeda; Bezrukova |
| **Scenario-based**, named-context vignettes (RG offices, branch, client-facing, online channels) | Plausible scenarios > abstract rules | EEOC; Clark |
| **Separate manager track** — disclosure handling, investigation triggers, reasonable steps | Role-specific dilemmas | EEOC §V.C.3; Worker Protection Act 2024 EHRC guidance |
| **SMCR-certified additional track** for FCA-Senior-Manager-regime staff | Personal regulatory accountability for culture under SMCR | FCA Non-Financial Misconduct policy statement |
| **Active-bystander 5Ds framing**, not "don't be a harasser" framing | Reduces reactance; broadens the trained-behaviour-set | Right To Be; Green Dot evidence |
| **Reporting routes practiced** — at least one module ends with the learner walking through Speak Up / EHRC / Acas / direct-to-NED routes | EEOC: "test the reporting system" | EEOC Task Force §V |
| **CEO sign-off** on the certificate; named EHRC-style "reasonable steps" commitment | Leader visibility ranked highest by EEOC | EEOC; Worker Protection Act 2024 |
| **Effectiveness measured by reporting and complainant satisfaction**, not % completed | Completion is a vanity metric — Dobbin & Kalev | HBR 2016 |
| **Voluntary uplift modules** alongside mandatory base | Voluntary modules outperform mandatory ones | HBR 2016 |
| **Anonymous reflection prompts**, free-text, logged, never scored | Generation effect + avoids backlash + survives moral-licensing | Slamecka & Graf; BIT |

## 9. Design implications for TogetherBook

1. **Re-attribute the CEO's reference.** Replace "the IFS findings" in any briefing with "Dobbin & Kalev (HBR 2016) and the EEOC Select Task Force (2016)" — these are the actual sources, and they are *stronger* than a fictional IFS line.
2. **Replace the annual marathon with quarterly micro-doses.** Four short modules per fiscal year beats one 60-minute block; this is the single highest-leverage change and is grounded in spacing-effect data.
3. **Frame every module around the 5Ds, not around the harasser archetype.** This sidesteps reactance and broadens the trained behaviour set to the 95 % of staff who will never harass but who do witness incidents.
4. **Ship a separate Manager track.** Different scenarios (disclosure handling, "reasonable steps" decisions, escalation), shipped to anyone on the Directory whose Line-Manager-of field is non-empty. The Manager track is the EEOC's strongest recommendation and aligns with the Worker Protection Act 2024 EHRC guidance.
5. **Add an SMCR-certified additional track** for any RG staff member who is Certified or Senior-Manager-regime. Make it explicit that under SMCR they are personally accountable for culture, not just policy.
6. **CEO sign-off on every certificate.** The certificate page must show the CEO's name and a one-line "reasonable steps" commitment, not just a tick. EEOC: "must start with and involve the highest level of management."
7. **Reflection prompts must be open-text, logged, never scored.** Dobbin & Kalev's data shows engagement-led structures beat tested-content-only structures; the Slamecka generation-effect literature shows free-text recall outperforms forced-choice. Logging (not scoring) creates accountability without triggering reactance.
8. **Stop reporting completion %; start reporting (i) disclosure rate, (ii) median time-to-report, (iii) complainant-satisfaction survey score post-resolution.** These are the EEOC's "test the reporting system" metrics. Completion % is a vanity metric and Dobbin & Kalev showed it is *uncorrelated* with outcome change.
9. **Be ready for reported incidents to rise, not fall, in year one.** That is the literature's clearest empirical pattern when reporting routes start working. Brief the Board on this before launch so a rise isn't read as failure.
10. **Practise the reporting route inside the module.** Every learner must, at least once, click through the actual Speak Up / EHRC / Acas links inside the training, not just be told they exist. EEOC explicitly: "test their reporting system."
11. **Tag content to context.** Branch staff, contact-centre staff, head-office staff and field underwriters face different harassment risks. Re-use a common scaffold but swap the vignettes — Bezrukova's meta-analysis flags tailoring as one of three effectiveness moderators.
12. **Avoid framing men as the problem and women as the protected class.** PNAS 2019 (Dobbin & Kalev) shows that frame produces the Pence-effect backlash and gender-differential worsening of judgement. Use mixed-gender, mixed-direction scenarios including same-gender, third-party-customer, and online channels.
13. **Add a voluntary "deeper" tier** — short interviews, allyship reading, or a culture-action group sign-up — alongside the mandatory base. Dobbin & Kalev's 9–13 % representation gains came from voluntary engagement, not from mandatory content.
14. **Annual independent review.** Publish, internally, the year-on-year metrics (disclosures, time-to-report, satisfaction) alongside a single page of "what we changed in response." This is the accountability-structure mechanism Kalev/Dobbin/Kelly (2006) identified as the *only* category of intervention that reliably moves the needle.
