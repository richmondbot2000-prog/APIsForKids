# FCA Non-Financial Misconduct & SMCR: Research Briefing

**Prepared**: 20 May 2026
**Scope**: Primary-source briefing for a UK financial-services group designing a harassment-prevention training module.

---

## 1. The current rules: Policy Statement PS25/23

The most recent — and now definitive — policy instrument is **PS25/23: Tackling non-financial misconduct in financial services**, published by the FCA on **12 December 2025** ([FCA PS25/23 landing page](https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/policy-statements/ps25-23-tackling-non-financial-misconduct-financial-services); [PDF](https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/policy/ps25-23.pdf)). PS25/23 supersedes the consultation track that ran through CP23/20 ("Diversity and inclusion in the financial sector", September 2023) and CP25/18 ("Tackling non-financial misconduct in financial services", July 2025).

**What PS25/23 actually does:**

- Introduces a new Handbook rule, **COCON 1.1.7FR**, that extends the Conduct Rules to bullying, harassment and violence against colleagues in **all SMCR-regulated firms**, not just banks. From **1 September 2026**, the rule applies to approximately **37,000 additional firms** beyond the banking population already in scope ([FCA press release, 2 July 2025](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/press-releases/fca-expectations-bullying-harassment-violence-deepen-trust-financial-services)).
- Aligns the seriousness threshold with the **Equality Act 2010** test: "unwanted conduct that has the purpose or effect of violating a person's dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment for the individual or conduct that is violent to the individual."
- Adds **non-Handbook guidance** to the **FIT** sourcebook explaining how non-financial misconduct — including private-life conduct in some cases — feeds into a Fit and Proper determination.

**Consultation history**: CP23/20 attracted strong support for the NFM strand (around 80% of authorised firms in favour); the rest of the D&I package was dropped in March 2025. CP25/18 took the NFM proposals forward in isolation; **95% of respondents agreed with the FCA's proposed guidance** ([FCA news, 12 December 2025](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/news-stories/fca-confirms-final-guidance-tackle-non-financial-misconduct)).

**Material extract — FCA's stated purpose**: "The primary responsibility for preventing and dealing with non-financial misconduct lies with firms… [the guidance] will help promote healthy and inclusive workplace cultures and deepen trust in financial services, in line with our strategy for 2025 to 2030."

## 2. Conduct Rules and non-financial misconduct

Under the existing Code of Conduct (COCON), every SMF and certified person is bound by the Individual Conduct Rules — most directly **ICR 1 (act with integrity)** and **ICR 2 (act with due skill, care and diligence)**. Pre-2026, the FCA's position was that harassment could amount to a breach of ICR 1 for banks; for non-banks it was ambiguous.

**COCON 1.1.7FR removes that ambiguity from 1 September 2026.** Bullying, harassment or violence against a colleague — where there is a sufficient nexus to the individual's role — is in scope of COCON for **every** SMCR firm. The FCA explicitly lists seriousness factors firms must weigh (COCON 4.3.8 guidance): whether the conduct is repeated or part of a pattern; duration; impact on the subject; seniority of the perpetrator; power differential; prior warnings; whether it would justify dismissal; criminal character.

**Whistleblowing dimension**: The FCA's existing handbook obligation sits in **SYSC 18** — firms must "establish, implement and maintain appropriate and effective arrangements for the disclosure of reportable concerns by whistleblowers" (SYSC 18.3.1R). The FCA explicitly confirmed in 2024 that **NDAs cannot prevent disclosures to the FCA, other regulators or law enforcement** ([Culture and non-financial misconduct survey findings, 25 October 2024](https://www.fca.org.uk/data/culture-non-financial-misconduct-survey-findings)). There is no document called "FG23/3 whistleblowing" — that reference number belongs to cryptoasset financial promotions guidance. The operative whistleblowing instruments remain SYSC 18 and the FCA's whistleblower webpages.

**2023–2024 enforcement messaging**: The FCA's Culture & NFM survey of 1,028 wholesale firms (February 2024, results published October 2024) is the most important empirical baseline. Headline findings: bullying and harassment made up **26%** of all reported NFM incidents; discrimination **23%**. Concerningly, **47% of bullying/harassment allegations and 62% of discrimination allegations were not upheld** on investigation — suggesting either a sector-wide investigation-quality issue or under-substantiation; either way it became the FCA's evidence base for new rules.

## 3. Fit and Propriety under SMCR

The Fit and Proper test sits in the **FIT** sourcebook. **FIT 2.1 (Honesty, integrity and reputation)** has always allowed the FCA — and obliges firms — to consider "any matter which may have an adverse impact on the individual's reputation," including non-financial conduct.

PS25/23 adds non-Handbook guidance (FIT 1.3.17G–18G in the consulted text) confirming:

- A **substantiated finding of serious harassment or bullying** is directly relevant to F&P. Relevant factors track FIT 2.1.3G: seriousness of the conduct; whether dishonesty, breach of trust or violence is involved; rehabilitation; whether the conduct was repeated or patterned.
- **Private-life conduct** can be relevant if it shows "a willingness to disregard ethical or legal obligations, abuse a position of trust or exploit the vulnerabilities of others" or is sufficiently serious to "undermine public confidence in the regulatory system." Firms are **not** expected to monitor private lives, but when matters come to their attention they "should investigate reasonably."
- Firms must **retain documented records** showing how NFM was considered in any certification or re-attestation decision. The audit trail is itself a regulatory requirement under SYSC record-keeping and the certification regime — the absence of one is independently problematic.

**The Odey precedent**: On 3 March 2025 the FCA issued a Final Notice against Crispin Odey, fining him **£1.8 million** and prohibiting him from the UK financial services industry. The breach was of **Individual Conduct Rule 1 (integrity)** — specifically for "deliberately seek[ing] to frustrate ongoing disciplinary processes" into sexual-harassment allegations against him. The case is currently before the Upper Tribunal (hearing commenced 10 March 2026) — but the Decision Notice is itself a clear marker of FCA appetite to use F&P / Conduct Rule tools against senior figures over harassment-adjacent conduct.

## 4. Reporting obligations

Three regimes interlock — firms commonly confuse them:

1. **SUP 10C.14 — 7-day clock for SMFs.** Where a firm becomes aware that an **approved Senior Manager** has breached a Conduct Rule (including, post-September 2026, by serious harassment), the firm must notify the FCA via **Form D** within **7 business days**. Where the SMF ceases to perform the role — including suspension, dismissal or resignation in connection with the matter — the firm must submit **Form C**.

2. **SUP 15.11 / REP008 (Form H) — annual reporting for non-SMFs.** Conduct Rule breaches by **certified persons** and other COCON-staff (i.e. everyone other than SMFs) are reported **annually** via REP008, which is the GABRIEL/RegData return populated by Form H (SUP 15 Annex 7R). Reporting period 1 September to 31 August; **submission deadline 31 October** ([FCA REP008 page](https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/senior-managers-certification-regime/rep008-solo-regulated-firms)). Reportable disciplinary actions are: formal written warnings, suspension or dismissal, and remuneration reduction or recovery.

3. **SUP 15.3 — general notification under Principle 11.** This is the catch-all. Principle 11 requires firms to deal with regulators "in an open and cooperative way" and to disclose "appropriately anything relating to the firm of which the FCA would reasonably expect notice." SUP 15.3.1R operationalises this. A serious NFM incident — for example, a credible allegation of harassment against a senior executive, a media disclosure, or a pattern of complaints — can trigger a SUP 15.3 notification even where the formal 7-day SMF clock has not yet started.

**Regulatory references (SYSC 22)**: from 1 September 2026, substantiated NFM findings must be disclosed in regulatory references when the individual moves firm — closing the "pass the harasser" route that the FCA has repeatedly flagged.

## 5. Recent FCA enforcement and public statements (2023–2026)

**Sarah Pritchard, FCA Deputy Chief Executive — 2 July 2025 press release** ([FCA](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/press-releases/fca-expectations-bullying-harassment-violence-deepen-trust-financial-services)):

> "Too often when we see problems in the market, there are cultural failings in firms. Behaviour like bullying or harassment going unchallenged is one of the reddest flags — a culture where this occurs can raise questions about a firm's decision making and risk management. Our new rules will help drive consistency across industry and support the vast majority of firms that want to do the right thing to deepen trust in financial services."

**Sarah Pritchard — Treasury Select Committee, "Sexism in the City" inquiry (early 2024)**: "The industry is very much asking us to lean in for some regulatory intervention" ([DLA Piper analysis](https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2024/02/sexism-in-the-city-the-latest-fca-spotlight-on-non-financial-misconduct)).

**Therese Chambers, Joint Executive Director of Enforcement and Market Oversight — January 2026** confirmed publicly the FCA "does not rule out enforcement action over non-financial misconduct," reiterating the Odey approach as a template ([WilmerHale, FCA enforcement trends 2025/2026](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/blogs/wilmerhale-w-i-r-e-uk/20260109-fca-enforcement-trends-in-2025-and-expectations-for-2026)).

**FCA institutional position (PS25/23, December 2025)**: "Failure to tackle toxic behaviour drives away good people, prevents staff from speaking up and undermines performance. It damages growth and enables financial misconduct."

**Survey baseline (October 2024)**: 26% of reported NFM incidents were bullying/harassment; 23% discrimination; settlement-and-confidentiality agreements were used most in discrimination cases (124 over 2021–2023).

## 6. The "FCA dimension" — plain-English copy for Module 1

> If you work for an FCA-regulated firm, your career sits on top of a second layer of permission the regulator can take away. Every certified person and Senior Manager has to be reassessed as "fit and proper" each year — and from September 2026, a substantiated finding of harassment, bullying or violence can be the thing that fails that test. That is not an HR sanction. It is a regulatory finding that follows you between employers, gets disclosed in regulatory references, and in serious cases can end with the FCA prohibiting you from working in financial services at all. The Crispin Odey decision — a £1.8 million fine and an industry ban over conduct around harassment allegations — is the public marker that the regulator is willing to use these powers. The point isn't fear; the point is that the rules people learned for school or for ordinary jobs do not capture the full downside. In our industry, behaviour is a licensing matter.

---

## Design implications

- **Module 1 must open on the F&P consequence, not on the legal definition.** The single most powerful lever generic training misses is: a finding here can end a regulated career. Lead with it.
- **Anchor the regime change date (1 September 2026) explicitly.** Staff in non-bank entities need to know the rule is new to them and is not a re-statement of existing HR policy.
- **Use the FCA's own definition verbatim** (the Equality Act-aligned wording from COCON 1.1.7FR) so the test in training matches the test the firm will apply in a disciplinary process.
- **Teach the seriousness factors from COCON 4.3.8 as a checklist** — pattern, duration, impact, seniority, power differential, prior warnings. This converts a vague "is it serious?" into something a line manager or witness can actually evaluate.
- **Cover the reporting architecture in one slide**: 7-day Form D for SMFs (SUP 10C.14); annual REP008/Form H for certified persons (SUP 15.11); SUP 15.3 Principle 11 catch-all for anything serious enough that the FCA "would reasonably expect notice." Staff don't need to memorise it but should know reports go to the regulator.
- **Address the "private life" misunderstanding head-on.** Conduct outside work can be relevant to F&P; the firm will not monitor private lives but it must act when matters come to its attention.
- **Quote Sarah Pritchard's "reddest flags" line.** It is the punchiest line the regulator has put on record and shifts the framing from compliance to culture.
- **Reference the Odey Final Notice as a concrete worked example** — not for shock value, but because it is the public proof that the FCA will treat conduct around harassment allegations (even at the most senior levels) as a regulatory matter.
- **Cover regulatory references and the "pass the harasser" problem.** Staff should understand that substantiated findings move with the individual; the FCA closed that loophole deliberately.
- **End with the speak-up obligation.** SYSC 18 whistleblowing protections and the FCA's confirmation that NDAs cannot block disclosures to the regulator should be the closing message — the corollary of strong consequences is robust protected reporting.
