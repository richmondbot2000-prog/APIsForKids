# UK Harassment-Prevention Training: Legal Framework Research

**Prepared 20 May 2026 for design of a corporate training module covering UK law on workplace harassment, the sexual-harassment preventative duty, third-party harassment, and whistleblowing.**

---

## 1. Equality Act 2010 — the core framework

**URL:** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/15
**In force since:** 1 October 2010 (most substantive provisions)

### Section 4 — the nine protected characteristics

Section 4 EA 2010 enumerates the nine protected characteristics: **age; disability; gender reassignment; marriage and civil partnership; pregnancy and maternity; race; religion or belief; sex; sexual orientation.** Section 26(5) limits the harassment provisions to seven of these (marriage/civil partnership and pregnancy/maternity are excluded from harassment but remain protected against direct discrimination).

### Section 26 — definition of harassment

Section 26(1) reads verbatim:

> "A person (A) harasses another (B) if— (a) A engages in unwanted conduct related to a relevant protected characteristic, and (b) the conduct has the purpose or effect of— (i) violating B's dignity, or (ii) creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment for B."

Section 26(2) defines **sexual harassment** specifically as "unwanted conduct of a sexual nature" with the same purpose/effect. Section 26(3) covers less-favourable treatment for rejecting/submitting to such conduct. Section 26(4) tells tribunals to consider: **B's perception, the other circumstances of the case, and whether it is reasonable for the conduct to have that effect.**

### Section 27 — victimisation (retaliation)

A person victimises B if they subject B to detriment because B has done a "protected act": bringing proceedings, giving evidence/information, doing anything else for the purposes of the Act, or "making an allegation (whether or not express) that A or another person has contravened this Act." Bad-faith false allegations are excluded.

### Section 40 — workplace harassment (current text)

As currently in force (May 2026), section 40(1) reads:

> "An employer (A) must not, in relation to employment by A, harass a person (B)— (a) who is an employee of A's; (b) who has applied to A for employment."

The original third-party-harassment subsections (40(2)–(4)) were **repealed on 1 October 2013** by the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013. New third-party subsections (1A)–(1C) inserted by the Employment Rights Act 2025 are **not yet in force as of today (20 May 2026)** — they commence in October 2026 (see §3).

**Training implication:** the module must teach the s.26 three-limb test (unwanted / related to characteristic / purpose-or-effect), and must explain that intent is irrelevant — only effect, judged through s.26(4).

## 2. Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023

**URL:** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/51/contents
**Royal Assent:** 26 October 2023. **In force:** 26 October 2024.

The Act does three things.

### (a) New section 40A — the preventative duty

Section 40A(1) Equality Act 2010 (inserted by s.1 of the 2023 Act) reads verbatim:

> "An employer (A) must take reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment of employees of A in the course of their employment."

Section 40A(2) defines sexual harassment by reference to s.26(2). Section 40A(3) makes a breach enforceable by the EHRC under Part 1 of the Equality Act 2006 and by employment tribunals via the new compensation-uplift route. **It is a free-standing duty:** an employer can breach it without any specific employee having been harassed — the EHRC can act on systemic failure.

### (b) Section 124A — the compensation uplift

If a tribunal finds sexual harassment and also finds the s.40A duty breached, it must order an uplift up to **25 %** of the compensation awarded under s.124(2)(b). The uplift is not optional once a breach is established — tribunals must consider it.

### (c) EHRC enforcement powers

The EHRC's existing Equality Act 2006 powers (investigations, unlawful-act notices, action plans, injunctions) extend to s.40A breaches. The EHRC can act **without any individual complainant**.

**URL (EHRC press release, 26 September 2024):** https://www.equalityhumanrights.com/media-centre/news/ehrc-publishes-updated-workplace-sexual-harassment-guidance-ahead-change-law

**Training implication:** the duty is **anticipatory and proactive**. Doing nothing is not an option. Even if no employee complains, the EHRC alone can investigate and enforce. The 25% uplift is a board-level financial risk on top of the underlying harassment award.

## 3. Employment Rights Act 2025 — status as of 20 May 2026

**URL:** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/36/contents
**Royal Assent:** 18 December 2025.

ACAS confirms (https://www.acas.org.uk/employment-rights-act-2025, last updated 7 April 2026): "The employment law changes included in the Act will happen in 2026 and 2027. Most changes have not happened yet."

### Already in force (April 2026)

- **s.23 — Protection of disclosures relating to sexual harassment.** Commenced **6 April 2026** by S.I. 2026/323 reg. 3(1)(2). See §6 below.
- Sick pay from day one, day-one paternity leave, holiday-record requirements, Fair Work Agency establishment (all 6–7 April 2026).

### Coming into force October 2026 (NOT yet in force)

- **s.20 — Employers to take ALL reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment.** This amends s.40A(1) EA 2010 by inserting the word "all" before "reasonable steps". The threshold rises from "reasonable steps" to **"all reasonable steps"**.
- **s.21 — Harassment by third parties.** Inserts new s.40(1A)–(1C) EA 2010:

> "(1A) An employer (A) must not permit a third party to harass a person (B) who is an employee of A.
> (1B) [An employer permits harassment only where the third party harasses the employee in the course of employment AND the employer failed to take all reasonable steps to prevent it.]
> (1C) [A third party is any person other than the employer or their employees.]"

This **reinstates** third-party liability (originally repealed in 2013) and applies it to **all protected characteristics** — not only sexual harassment.

- **s.22 — power to make regulations specifying what counts as "reasonable steps".** The HM Government factsheet confirms regulations will be **published in 2027 after public consultation** (https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6968f4653f43dfed1544b0d8/preventing-workplace-sexual-harassment-enabling-regulations-about-reasonable-steps.pdf). Until then, the EHRC Technical Guidance is the operative reference point.
- **s.24 — restrictions on confidentiality clauses / NDAs** that prevent disclosure of harassment or discrimination.

**Sources:** Shoosmiths (https://www.shoosmiths.com/perspectives/stories/articles/2026-a-new-era-for-the-duty-to-prevent-sexual-harassment) and Pennington Manches (https://www.penningtonslaw.com/insights/harassment-and-the-employment-rights-act-2025-what-employers-need-to-know/) confirm October 2026 commencement; ACAS confirms regulatory clarification arrives in 2027.

**Training implication:** the module needs an "October 2026 readiness" section. The shift from "reasonable" to **"all** reasonable" is not cosmetic — Make UK, Bird & Bird and Pennington Manches all read it as requiring documented, systematic, evidence-based prevention rather than best-efforts. Third-party harassment liability must be taught explicitly because most employees in customer-facing financial-services roles encounter the public daily.

## 4. EHRC Technical Guidance + Eight-Step Employer Guide

**URLs:**
- Technical Guidance: https://www.equalityhumanrights.com/guidance/sexual-harassment-and-harassment-work-technical-guidance
- 8-step Employer Guide: https://www.equalityhumanrights.com/employer-8-step-guide-preventing-sexual-harassment-work
- Original Technical Guidance issued January 2020; **updated 26 September 2024** ahead of the 26 October 2024 commencement of s.40A.

The EHRC's framework for what is "reasonable" weighs:

- the **size and resources** of the employer
- the sector and the **nature of the contacts** (customers, clients, third parties)
- the **risks already identified** in the workplace
- the **time, cost and disruption** of a step against its likely benefit
- whether **concerns have previously been raised** and how they were handled
- compliance with **regulator standards** (e.g., FCA Conduct Rules for our sector)
- the **effectiveness** of measures already in place

The EHRC says no single step satisfies the duty; the test is whether the **combination** is reasonable.

### The Eight Steps (verbatim headings)

1. **Develop an effective anti-harassment policy** — must name protected characteristics, prohibited behaviour, the disciplinary consequences, third-party harassment, and be regularly reviewed and communicated.
2. **Engage your staff** — 1-to-1s, exit interviews, surveys, open-door routes, listening for the unsaid.
3. **Assess and take steps to reduce risk in your workplace** — the EHRC says an employer is "unlikely to comply with the preventative duty unless they carry out a risk assessment." Factors include power imbalances, lack of diversity, lone working, alcohol, customer-facing roles, work events.
4. **Reporting** — multiple confidential channels, including anonymous; documented records of formal and informal concerns; non-employee reporting accepted.
5. **Training** — staff trained on what harassment is, how to report, how to intervene as a bystander, what managers must do on receipt; **regular refreshers**; content **tailored** to role/seniority; effectiveness monitored.
6. **Handle harassment complaints** — promptly, fairly, confidentially; protect the complainant from victimisation; act on findings.
7. **Dealing with third-party harassment** — treat it as seriously as colleague harassment; warn third parties; act on patterns (e.g., known problem customer).
8. **Monitor and evaluate your actions** — anonymous surveys, complaint-data review, willingness to change what isn't working.

The EHRC stresses these steps are **not binding** but tribunals "will likely scrutinise" employer efforts against them. They are also **not exhaustive** — sector-specific steps may be needed.

**Training implication:** the module itself is Step 5. Crucially, however, the module must reference Steps 1, 3, 4, 6, 7 and 8 so learners understand the system within which their training sits. Risk assessment (Step 3) is now treated as a near-mandatory predicate — the module should note that the firm has carried one out.

## 5. ACAS guidance

**URLs:**
- Sexual harassment: https://www.acas.org.uk/sexual-harassment
- Harassment generally: https://www.acas.org.uk/if-youre-treated-unfairly-at-work/being-harassed (last updated 27 March 2025)
- Bullying: https://www.acas.org.uk/if-youre-treated-unfairly-at-work/being-bullied (last updated 16 December 2025)
- Employment Rights Act 2025 tracker: https://www.acas.org.uk/employment-rights-act-2025

ACAS mirrors the s.26 statutory tests and the seven harassment-relevant protected characteristics. It is more explicit than the EHRC on two points:

- **Intent is irrelevant** — conduct can be unlawful even if unintended.
- **Vicarious liability** — employers are responsible for worker actions unless they prove statutory defence, which under the new duty effectively means showing all reasonable preventative steps.
- **Constructive dismissal risk** — a worker who resigns over an intolerable harassment environment can claim constructive unfair dismissal.

ACAS distinguishes **bullying** (no specific statute, but actionable as constructive dismissal/discrimination if it touches a protected characteristic) from **harassment** (statutory under the EA 2010). The training should cover this because most workplace conduct issues raised by employees use the word "bullying" — and managers need to recognise when bullying crosses the line into harassment.

**Training implication:** include ACAS's framing on vicarious liability and the bully/harassment line. ACAS is the body workers will be told to consult before tribunal — early-conciliation gatekeeping. The module should signpost the ACAS Helpline (0300 123 1100) as a route.

## 6. April 2026 whistleblowing reform — now in force

**URL:** https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/36/section/23
**Commencement:** **6 April 2026**, per S.I. 2026/323, reg. 3(1)(2).
**Mondaq summary (authoritative):** https://www.mondaq.com/uk/employee-rights-labour-relations/1770518/whistleblowing-protections-for-sexual-harassment-from-6-april-2026
**HMG factsheet:** https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/695fc7f1714f11cb497765eb/strengthening-protections-for-whistleblowers-factsheet.pdf

Section 23 ERA 2025 inserts a new paragraph **(da)** into s.43B(1) Employment Rights Act 1996, expanding "qualifying disclosure" to include disclosures tending to show:

> "that sexual harassment has occurred, is occurring or is likely to occur"

within the meaning of s.26(2) Equality Act 2010. A consequential definition is inserted at s.43L(1).

**Critical caveat:** the **"public interest"** requirement in s.43B is unchanged. A worker disclosing only their own individual sexual-harassment experience may not automatically be a whistleblower — they must "reasonably believe" the disclosure is in the public interest. Disclosures about patterns of conduct, repeat offenders, multiple victims, or systemic failures will more readily qualify.

A worker subjected to detriment or dismissal because they made such a disclosure can bring a tribunal claim with **uncapped compensation** under the whistleblowing regime, alongside any Equality Act claim.

**Training implication:** Managers must be trained that **any sexual-harassment complaint may now be a protected disclosure**. Mishandling — even in good faith — exposes the firm to whistleblowing-detriment claims with uncapped damages. This is a step-change in manager risk.

## 7. What "reasonable steps" / "all reasonable steps" actually means

Drawing on s.40A EA 2010, the EHRC Technical Guidance, the EHRC 8-step guide, the HMG factsheet on the s.22 regulation power, Bird & Bird's one-year review (https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/uk/the-sexual-harassment-prevention-duty-one-year-of-reasonable-steps-(and-more-steps-coming)), and post-October-2024 tribunal practice, the factors tribunals weigh are:

1. **Existence and content of a written anti-harassment policy** — including third-party harassment, named protected characteristics, sanctions.
2. **Communication** of the policy — induction, intranet, periodic reminders. Email-only push has been called out as insufficient.
3. **Training delivered, to whom, and how recently** — tribunals look for substantive content (not "tick-box"), tailored to role and seniority, with **refresher cycles**. A 2025 tribunal applied the 25% uplift where the employer had policies but no actual training on them and failed to investigate.
4. **Risk assessment** — documented, considering power imbalance, lone working, alcohol, customer contact, social events, hybrid/remote settings, sector-specific risks (financial services: client entertaining, branch settings, lone home visits).
5. **Reporting channels** — multiple, confidential, anonymised option, accessible to non-employees, escalation routes that bypass the alleged perpetrator's line manager.
6. **Investigation and response** — speed, fairness, protection of complainant from victimisation, consistent sanction.
7. **Monitoring** — anonymised survey data, complaint-trend analysis, exit-interview themes.
8. **Board / senior accountability** — named senior owner of the issue, board-level reporting cadence, learning loops after each complaint.
9. **Third-party arrangements** — visitor notices, contractor and supplier conduct clauses, warning/banning powers, escalation routes for customer-perpetrated harassment.
10. **Action taken after past concerns** — repeat issues without escalation will sink the defence.

The "all" in the 2026 standard tightens the test to: **can the employer point to any further step it could reasonably have taken?** That requires documented decision-making about steps considered and rejected, not just steps adopted.

## Design implications for the training module

1. **Cover all seven harassment-relevant protected characteristics** (age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation) — not just sex/sexual harassment. The October 2026 third-party regime applies to all of them.
2. **Teach the verbatim s.26(1) three-limb test** (unwanted / related to characteristic / purpose-or-effect violating dignity or creating an environment). Use s.26(4)'s reasonableness lens to defuse "but they didn't mean it" defences — intent is irrelevant.
3. **Distinguish bullying from harassment** explicitly, per ACAS, because workers conflate them.
4. **Include sexual harassment as a stand-alone category** with the s.26(2) definition, since it carries its own preventative duty, uplift, and (from April 2026) whistleblowing protection.
5. **Teach victimisation under s.27** — including bystanders and witnesses. The module is partly a defence against retaliation claims.
6. **Explain the s.40A duty plainly:** the firm has a free-standing legal duty to prevent sexual harassment whether or not anything has happened. This is not just risk management — it is a statute.
7. **Cover the 25 % uplift** (s.124A) so employees see the financial stakes; cover EHRC enforcement so they understand the firm can be investigated without a complainant.
8. **Build in third-party harassment explicitly** — because from October 2026, s.40(1A)–(1C) ERA 2025 makes the firm directly liable for harassment by customers, contractors, suppliers, visitors. Customer-facing roles must learn the firm's escalation route for client/customer misconduct.
9. **State the shift to "ALL reasonable steps" from October 2026** so the training itself is current beyond October.
10. **Train managers separately** on receipt of complaints — investigation duty, confidentiality, victimisation prevention, escalation. EHRC Step 6 treats this as a distinct competency.
11. **Cover the April 2026 whistleblowing change** — every sexual-harassment complaint may now be a protected disclosure, with uncapped damages for detriment/dismissal. Managers must treat raising-the-concern itself as a protected act.
12. **Bystander module** — EHRC Step 2 ("engage your staff") and the cultural-prevention emphasis mean active bystander training is now expected. Tribunals look for it.
13. **Record completion centrally** with date and version — tribunals scrutinise recency. Provide refresher cadence (recommend annual minimum, given EHRC 8-step Step 5 and the "all" threshold).
14. **Tailor content to role/seniority** — the EHRC and Bird & Bird both flag generic e-learning as a weakness. Build segmented modules for managers, customer-facing staff, and senior leaders.
15. **Reference the firm's policy, reporting routes, and risk assessment** inside the module — so the training is evidently embedded in a wider system (Steps 1, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8 of the EHRC framework). A standalone module is unlikely to be "all reasonable steps" on its own.
16. **Add a board-oversight statement** — name the senior executive accountable, the reporting cadence, and confirm board-level review of complaint trends. This is the single highest-value evidential point for an EHRC investigation.
