## 1. The UK legal framework, as of today

The training has to be defensible against **four overlapping regimes**. Treat them as a stack — each layer raises the bar:

**a. Equality Act 2010 (the foundation).**
- **Section 26** defines harassment: unwanted conduct *related to a protected characteristic* with the *purpose or effect* of violating dignity or creating an intimidating environment. Section 26(4) says intent is irrelevant — only effect, judged through a reasonableness lens.
- **Section 27** — victimisation (retaliating against someone who complains) is itself unlawful, even if the underlying complaint isn't upheld.
- **Nine protected characteristics**, seven of which trigger the harassment provisions: age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation. (Marriage/civil partnership and pregnancy/maternity sit outside s.26 but remain protected against discrimination.)

**b. Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023 (already in force).**
- New **s.40A**: employer must take **reasonable steps** to prevent sexual harassment. This is a *free-standing duty* — the EHRC can investigate and enforce without any individual complainant.
- New **s.124A**: tribunals **must** consider a compensation uplift of up to **25%** where the duty has been breached.

**c. Employment Rights Act 2025 — in force April 2026, more in October 2026.**
- **Already live (6 April 2026):** s.23 ERA 2025 makes any qualifying sexual-harassment disclosure a **protected whistleblowing disclosure** under the Employment Rights Act 1996. Detriment or dismissal in response is compensable with **uncapped damages**, on top of any Equality Act claim. The public-interest test still applies, so an individual personal grievance may not qualify — but anything about a pattern, repeat offender, or systemic failure will.
- **Coming 1 October 2026 (s.20):** the duty becomes **all reasonable steps**. The word "all" tightens the test materially — tribunals will ask "could the firm point to any further step it could reasonably have taken?"
- **Coming 1 October 2026 (s.21):** third-party harassment liability reinstated. The firm becomes directly liable for harassment of its employees by **clients, contractors, suppliers, visitors** — and across *all* protected characteristics, not just sex.

**d. EHRC framework (the operative reference).**
- The EHRC's **Eight Steps** for employers are the de facto checklist tribunals weigh: policy · staff engagement · risk assessment · multiple reporting channels · training · complaint handling · third-party arrangements · monitoring & evaluation.
- The EHRC explicitly says no single step satisfies the duty. The test is whether the *combination* is reasonable.
- The October 2026 regulations under s.22 ERA 2025 (defining "reasonable steps" in detail) won't land until 2027. Until then, the EHRC Technical Guidance (last updated September 2024) is the operative reference.

→ Full citations + verbatim primary sources: `training/research/01_uk_legal_framework.md`.

## 2. The FCA layer — the part generic training misses

This is the lever no off-the-shelf provider executes well, and it's the most powerful "why should I care" argument for individuals in financial services.

**PS25/23 (12 December 2025).** New Handbook rule **COCON 1.1.7FR** extends the Conduct Rules to bullying, harassment and violence against colleagues across **all SMCR-regulated firms** from **1 September 2026**. The seriousness threshold is the Equality Act test — verbatim. Approximately 37,000 additional firms enter scope beyond banks.

**Fitness and Propriety.** PS25/23 also clarifies FIT 2.1: a substantiated finding of serious harassment or bullying is directly relevant to F&P. Private-life conduct *can* matter where it "shows a willingness to disregard ethical or legal obligations, abuse a position of trust or exploit the vulnerabilities of others." Firms are not expected to surveil private lives but must investigate reasonably when matters surface.

**Reporting architecture (the three regimes that interlock):**
- **SUP 10C.14** — SMF Conduct Rule breaches reported via Form D within **7 business days**.
- **REP008 / Form H** — certified-person breaches reported annually by 31 October (period 1 Sep – 31 Aug).
- **SUP 15.3 / Principle 11** — catch-all "you would reasonably expect notice" notification for anything serious enough.
- **SYSC 22 regulatory references** — from 1 September 2026, substantiated NFM findings must be disclosed in references when an individual moves firm. This closes the "pass the harasser" loophole.

**Sarah Pritchard, FCA Deputy Chief Executive (2 July 2025):**
> "Behaviour like bullying or harassment going unchallenged is one of the reddest flags — a culture where this occurs can raise questions about a firm's decision making and risk management."

**The Odey precedent (3 March 2025):** £1.8m fine + industry ban under ICR 1 (integrity) for conduct around the handling of sexual-harassment allegations. Currently under Upper Tribunal review but the Decision Notice is the public proof that the FCA will use these tools at the most senior level.

**The one line that should anchor Module 1's opening:**

> If you work for an FCA-regulated firm, your career sits on top of a second layer of permission the regulator can take away. From September 2026, a substantiated finding of harassment, bullying or violence can be the thing that fails the annual fit-and-proper test. That's not an HR sanction. It's a regulatory finding that follows you between employers, gets disclosed in references, and in serious cases can end with the FCA prohibiting you from working in financial services at all.

→ Full citations + statute references: `training/research/02_fca_smcr.md`.

## 3. What "reasonable steps / all reasonable steps" actually means in practice

Synthesising the EHRC framework, the Bird & Bird "one year of the preventative duty" review, and post-October-2024 tribunal practice, the factors tribunals weigh are:

1. Existence + content of a written policy (including third-party harassment).
2. **How** the policy is communicated (email-only push has been called out as insufficient).
3. **Training delivered, to whom, and how recently** — substantive, not tick-box, with refresher cycles.
4. **Risk assessment** — documented, considering power imbalance, lone working, alcohol, customer contact, social events, hybrid/remote, sector specifics.
5. Reporting channels — multiple, confidential, anonymised option, escalation bypassing the alleged perpetrator's line.
6. Investigation + response — speed, fairness, protection from victimisation, consistent sanction.
7. Monitoring — anonymised survey data, complaint trends, exit-interview themes.
8. **Board / senior accountability** — named senior owner, board-level reporting cadence, learning loops after each complaint.
9. Third-party arrangements — visitor notices, contractor conduct clauses, escalation routes for customer harassment.
10. Action taken after past concerns.

The "all" upgrade in October 2026 means the firm must be able to **document the decisions it took about steps it considered and rejected**, not just the steps it adopted. That's an evidentiary record point, which TogetherBook (as the audit-trail system) is well-placed to provide.

## 4. What works, what doesn't — the training-effectiveness evidence

**An honest correction up front.** The "IFS findings" cited in the brief don't appear in the IFS catalogue. The misattribution doesn't matter for the argument — the *actual* sources are stronger and bigger. They are:

- **Dobbin & Kalev, "Why Diversity Programs Fail," HBR 2016** — 829-firm 30-year longitudinal dataset.
- **EEOC Select Task Force on Harassment, Feldblum & Lipnic 2016** — the most authoritative regulator review.
- **Bezrukova et al. 40-year meta-analysis, Psychological Bulletin 2016** — 260 independent samples.
- **Behavioural Insights Team — UBT/Diversity Training Evidence**.

**What they collectively show:**

- **Mandatory tick-box training, on its own, often makes things slightly worse.** Dobbin & Kalev: required management training was associated with a *9% decrease* in Black women in management over 5 years. Mechanism: psychological reactance ("don't tell me what to do") + moral licensing ("I've done the training, I've earned a pass").
- **Voluntary uplift programmes produced 9–13% representation gains** over the same period. Voluntary > mandatory, every time.
- **The single biggest moderator of effectiveness is structure, not content.** Accountability mechanisms (diversity task forces, mentoring, dedicated managers, board reporting) move the needle. Training and grievance systems alone don't.
- **The PNAS 2019 follow-up (Dobbin & Kalev)** found a gender-differential backlash: men exposed to traditional sexual-harassment training were *more* likely to blame victims and *less* likely to mentor junior women — the "Pence effect."
- **Bystander training has the strongest evidence base.** Green Dot multi-college RCT: 17–21% lower interpersonal-violence victimisation in trained vs control populations. UK BMJ Open community evaluation showed large self-efficacy + intent gains. The 5Ds framework (Direct, Distract, Delegate, Delay, Document) is now de facto standard across UK universities.
- **Spacing > marathon.** Four 15-minute doses across the year retain 2–3× longer than one 60-minute block. Decay is sharp.
- **Testing-on-recall > re-reading**, but only if the testing is genuine reasoning, not pure recall.
- **Manager tracks are the EEOC's single strongest recommendation.** Managers face role-specific dilemmas (disclosure handling, intervening across hierarchy) absent from generic content.
- **Be ready for incident reports to *rise* in year one**, not fall. That's the evidence-based pattern when reporting routes start working — and it's a board-comms point that has to happen before launch.

→ Full evidence base + citations: `training/research/04_training_effectiveness.md`.

## 5. Provider audit + UX/accessibility findings

**Provider scan headline.** The UK FS-specific players (Skillcast, Marshalls) have the regulatory hook but ship dry, thin, scenario-free microlearning. The polished US providers (Traliant, EVERFI/ComplyEQ) have the production values but don't speak the FCA's language. **Nobody combines both well.** That's the gap TogetherBook fills.

**Patterns to steal:**

- Oxford's **laddered architecture** — short e-learning + targeted micro-videos + in-person bystander workshop + advisor track. The best architecture in the audit.
- Traliant's **"no reruns" annual rotation** — year 2 questions ≠ year 1.
- iHASCO's **"grey area" sections** — scenarios where the answer is genuinely unclear.
- UCL's **content warnings + opt-out path** at the start of sexual-harassment scenarios.
- LSE's **disclosure-receiving framed as its own competency** for managers.
- Skillcast's **non-financial-misconduct framing** — "harassment IS misconduct, misconduct IS a fitness & propriety issue, fitness & propriety IS a regulatory matter." Reusable verbatim.

**Patterns to avoid:**

- **Forced-progression** ("must select correct answer to proceed"). EVERFI's main anti-pattern. Rewards persistence, not understanding.
- **Pure end-of-module multiple choice**. iHASCO's 20-question recall test — the "tick and flick" tell.
- **"Now let's meet Sarah" character intros.** Patronising on a trading floor.
- **Machine-translated DEI content.** Credibility-killing.
- **"Disclaimer: not legal advice" on every page.** One footer disclaimer, then stop.
- **Stock-photo cast in dress-up.** Either use real, named TogetherBook colleagues or use illustrated abstract scenarios. Never the uncanny-valley middle.

**UX bar.** The product to feel like — in tone, in typography, in restraint — is the Linear/Stripe/Notion class of internal tooling, not corporate-LMS-by-default. Specifically: Inter or similar neutral grotesque, restrained colour (one accent used as accent, not chrome), tabular numerals in any scored display, generous spacing, max line length ~70ch, body text ~16-18px, line-height 1.6-1.75.

**Microcopy bar.** Copy a thoughtful colleague would send.
- Bad: *"Woohoo, great job, champion!"*
- Good: *"Module 1 complete. Module 2 is now available."*
- Bad: *"Incorrect! Try again!"*
- Good: *"That's not the answer. Under the Equality Act 2010, indirect discrimination is when a policy applies equally to everyone but disadvantages a protected group. The correct answer is B."*

**WCAG 2.2 AA — the 15 criteria that materially affect this build:**

| Criterion | Build implication |
|---|---|
| 1.4.3 Contrast | Body text ≥ 4.5:1; `#52525b` minimum on white |
| 1.4.11 Non-text Contrast | Unfocused borders ≥ 3:1; `#a1a1aa` or darker |
| 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable | No per-question countdowns |
| 2.3.3 Animation from Interactions | `prefers-reduced-motion` honoured on every transition |
| 2.4.6 Headings & Labels | Each question is `<h2>`/`<h3>`; reflection box has `<label for>` |
| 2.4.7 Focus Visible | Never `outline: none`; 2px ring + 3px offset |
| 2.4.11 Focus Not Obscured (new in 2.2) | `scroll-padding-top` equal to sticky-header height |
| 2.4.13 Focus Appearance (stretch) | 2px ring minimum, 3:1 contrast |
| 2.5.7 Dragging Movements (new in 2.2) | Avoid drag question types; if used, also tap-select-tap-place |
| 2.5.8 Target Size (new in 2.2) | ≥ 44px tall on mobile; ≥ 8px gaps between footer controls |
| 3.1.3 Unusual Words | Legal terms wrapped in `<dfn>` with glossary popover |
| 3.3.1 / 3.3.3 Error Identification | Wrong-answer feedback explains *why*, not just "Incorrect" |
| 3.3.7 Redundant Entry (new in 2.2) | Cert uses SSO name/email; retakes prefill reflections |
| 3.3.8 Accessible Authentication (new in 2.2) | No captcha; rely on Cloudflare Access SSO |
| 4.1.3 Status Messages | All feedback toasts `role="status"`; never steal focus |

## 6. The design brief — what we build, based on the above

This is the Phase 2 input. Every choice here ties back to evidence cited above.

### 6.1 Module architecture

A **laddered set**, not a single annual marathon. Spaced doses across the year for retention. Tracks tagged to role so each person gets the right content.

| ID | Title | Audience | Length | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0** | Professional conduct in digital channels (Teams/Slack/WhatsApp/email) | All staff | ~5 min + test | One-off + annual refresh |
| **1** | The law and why it matters | All staff | ~12 min + 8Q test | Annual |
| **2** | Recognising harassment in an office setting | All staff | ~12 min + 8Q test | Annual, scenario rotation |
| **3** | Bystander action — the 5Ds | All staff | ~10 min + 8Q test | Annual, scenario rotation |
| **4** | Power dynamics + high-risk situations | All staff (manager-emphasis) | ~12 min + 8Q test | Annual |
| **5** | Reporting and what happens next | All staff | ~10 min + 8Q test | Annual |
| **M** | Manager add-on — receiving disclosures, investigation duty, not weaponising performance management | Line managers + SMCR-certified | ~20 min + 10Q test | On appointment + annual |

Each module: 25-question bank, 8 served per attempt, 80% pass mark (7 of 8), two retakes before HR escalation. Two reflection prompts per module (logged, not scored).

### 6.2 Content principles

1. **Open every module on the FCA F&P consequence**, not the legal definition. That's the lever generic training misses for finance.
2. **All seven harassment-relevant protected characteristics**, not just sex. Third-party regime applies to all of them from October 2026.
3. **Address the meta-fears directly** — "will speaking up cost me my bonus / my career?", "what if I'm wrong?". Don't bury them.
4. **Scenarios anchored in our actual contexts** — open-plan trading floor, broker calls, client lunches, off-sites, return from maternity, contractor on the floor, mortgage-completion drinks. Never "Sarah and John in the photocopy room."
5. **5Ds (Direct, Distract, Delegate, Delay, Document) as the bystander framework** for Module 3.
6. **Plain-English legal box** on every relevant screen citing the actual statute. One disclaimer in the footer, never on every page.
7. **Grey-area questions** where the answer is genuinely contested — show what a tribunal, the FCA, and a line manager would each weigh.
8. **Manager add-on covers** disclosure-receiving as a named skill, investigation duty, evidence preservation, the no-weaponising-performance-management rule, the duty to act without a formal complaint, third-party situations.
9. **Content warnings + an "I want to talk to someone first" route** on every sexual-harassment scenario (UCL pattern). The route must be a first-class button.

### 6.3 UX principles

1. **Linear/Stripe restraint, not corporate-LMS theatre.** Inter or similar; restrained colour; tabular numerals; max line length ~70ch.
2. **Progress is a clickable stepper** showing real state ("Section 2/5 · Q 3/8"), never a throbbing percentage.
3. **One question per screen on mobile.** ≥ 44px tap targets. Scroll restored after feedback panel renders.
4. **Inline 1–2 sentence explanation after every answer** (right and wrong). Reference the rule that applies, not just "Correct" / "Incorrect."
5. **Save-and-resume on every screen.** Visible "Saved 14:32" timestamp. On return: "Resume Module 2, Q4 of 8" with a single primary button.
6. **Reflection textboxes have an explicit "Your manager will not see this" caveat** and an autosave indicator. Free text, never scored.
7. **Certificate is a single-page PDF**: full name (from SSO), date, modules covered, signing officer (CEO), unique verification ID. Designed like something an employee would actually keep.
8. **WCAG 2.2 AA throughout.** All 15 criteria in §5 honoured; the four new-in-2.2 criteria called out explicitly in the engineering brief.

### 6.4 Records and audit

Every employee record captures:

- Who took what, when (server-side timestamp).
- Number of attempts per module.
- Final score per attempt.
- Time spent per module (helps spot click-through).
- Reflection-prompt responses (text, HR review only, never visible to line manager).
- Certificate ID + version of the content at the time of issue.
- Next-due date.
- SMCR status flag (snapshot at issue), business line, role, hire date.

**Exportable** in CSV + PDF, per-employee and per-cohort, for tribunal or EHRC evidence.

**Retention default 7 years**, configurable.

**Immutable audit log** of all admin actions on training records (who changed what, when, why).

### 6.5 Deadlines + notifications

- New starters auto-enrolled on day one, **30-day deadline**.
- All-staff annual cycle: **14-day deadline** from assignment. Reminders at 10, 5, 2 days remaining and on the overdue date.
- Line managers + SMCR-certified auto-enrolled in the Manager add-on on appointment, **14-day deadline**.
- After day 15 overdue: line manager and HR auto-notified. Reminder copy is friendly first, firmer later, never preachy.

### 6.6 Year-2 refresh — content rotation

Year-2 question banks must not be identical to year-1. Rotate the scenario context:
- Year 1: office / trading-floor scenarios.
- Year 2: remote / Teams / Slack / WhatsApp scenarios.
- Year 3: client-facing / third-party scenarios (the s.21 ERA 2025 dimension).

### 6.7 Effectiveness measurement (this is the metric set HR should report on)

**Not completion %** (that's a vanity metric). Instead:

1. Disclosure / report rate (with the literature's warning that this will *rise* in year one when routes start working).
2. Median time-to-report.
3. Complainant satisfaction survey score post-resolution.
4. Reflection-prompt themes (anonymised, qualitative) — a free signal of cultural distance.

## 7. Recommendations for the brief itself

Three small corrections to feed back before Phase 2 kicks off:

1. **The "IFS findings" citation should be reframed** to "Dobbin & Kalev (HBR 2016) and the EEOC Select Task Force (2016)." Both are stronger sources and both are exactly what the underlying argument relies on.
2. **The brief says "October 2024 — reasonable steps."** Correct — and that duty is already live. The October 2026 uplift to *all* reasonable steps + third-party liability is the change to flag in the launch comms.
3. **The April 2026 whistleblowing change is already in force** (since 6 April 2026, via S.I. 2026/323). The training and the Manager track should both teach this as live law, not future law.
